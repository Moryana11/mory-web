Chapter I. – The Ruins of Turayne

Turayne had once been a bustling and prosperous city, thriving on the Grassy Path - an ancient trade route winding lazily through mountains and deep forests. Its inns had once been crowded every night with travelers, mercenaries, and merchants seeking rest before continuing their journeys. The streets never truly fell silent. Even long after nightfall, laughter, the clinking of coins, and the voices of traders bargaining over rare goods brought from distant lands echoed through the marketplace. People believed that as long as the bloodline of powerful mages watched over the city — those who had devoted their lives to its protection — Turayne would never fall. But everything changed on the day of the last High mage’s funeral. That was the day the Shadowmages of the Sylvean Order attacked. War erupted — brutal and short — as the city’s remaining mages fought desperately against the invaders. Turayne fell, and only ruins remained, haunted by undead corrupted by twisted magic. At the heart of the Ruins of Turayne stood the shattered Temple of Tevent — one of the last places that still reflected the city’s long-faded glory. The stone arches of the ancient temple stretched toward the sky like petrified fingers of gods who had once ruled these lands. The entrance hall, once bright and filled with prayer, now lay silent and consumed by shadow. Time did not pass here — it had sunk its teeth into everything like mold. Dust covered every surface so thickly that each step sent swirling clouds rising into the air, whispering like remnants of forgotten prayers. The pillars were cracked, some collapsed entirely, others overgrown with black lichen pulsing with its own dark energy. The air carried the stench of rotting flesh and something indescribable that settled on the tongue like ash. Statues stared from every alcove — but their eyes had been gouged out, their faces torn apart. Some were so badly shattered that it was no longer possible to tell whom or what they had once depicted. Bodies lay scattered among the rubble and debris. Some did not move. Others… moved far too slowly. Undead. Silent and twisted, as if they had long forgotten the purpose of their own limbs. They slowly reached decayed hands before them, their hollow eyes searching for even the faintest trace of life. The temple had not spat them out — it held them. All of them remained here. Priests, pilgrims, healers… their souls trapped within the walls, the floors, the shadows. Waiting. Wind slipped through the shattered windows, but it carried no fresh air — only cold and the whispers of long-buried things that should never have been awakened again. In some parts of the temple, strange echoes could be heard, belonging to no footsteps and no voice. From somewhere in the distance came the crunch of shifting rubble — quiet, yet foreign. A step. Another. Then one more. For a brief moment, a silhouette flickered through the dark passage between two collapsed pillars. A shard of light spilling from a broken window brushed against the figure moving slowly and silently, as though examining every shadow and every crack around her. She looked as if she were searching for something — or trying to make certain she was alone. Her long, straight black hair swayed softly with each step, framing her pale skin like a dark waterfall. Her black eyes, deep and calm as the night sky, carried both determination and a quiet sorrow. The blue dress she wore had been marked by travel and dust, yet still retained traces of elegance. Rowena came from Castleton, a bustling port city alive with the restless rhythm of ships, markets, and trade. The scent of sea salt mingled with spices, exotic herbs, and fresh produce brought from distant lands. The streets overflowed with shouting merchants offering fabrics, precious jewelry, and mysterious artifacts. Castleton was a place where cultures, languages, and destinies from across the world intertwined. She loved the city with all her heart — it was not merely a place of opportunity, but a home that had given her both freedom and safety. Her parents, experienced merchants, managed a renowned family business specializing in medicinal herbs, many gathered from the surrounding forests. When she was eight years old, an old healer visited the city and sensed something extraordinary within her. He realized Rowena possessed a rare gift for healing magic. After a long discussion, her parents agreed to let the girl follow her destiny, and the healer took her to the Tower of Pure Light — a prestigious academy where magic was taught in its purest and most powerful forms. At the Tower of Pure Light, she studied healing rituals, ancient scrolls, and the secrets of the Garden of Peace, all meant to one day make her capable of aiding the suffering. Every day she trained tirelessly, sharpening her abilities while carrying both the burden of responsibility and the weight of hope within her. In her hand, she tightly gripped a wand adorned with delicate runes, and at her waist hung a magical book — an ancient tome filled with rituals capable of changing the fate of many lives. Every step she took within Tevent’s Temple felt like sinking deeper into a swamp of memories, a place where past and present merged into a single dark current. Each breath was heavy and painful, as though the very air of the ruins refused to enter her lungs. Yet she kept moving forward — for the scrolls, for hope. Because even where everything had long since died, the key to saving the living might still remain. Her mind was sharp, and her heart held firm to the belief that magic was not merely a weapon, but a light capable of illuminating even the darkest corners of the world. And then she felt it. Not the chill of the wind, but another kind entirely. The kind that crawls down your spine when something without eyes is watching you. Ghosts. Temple priests — shapeless remnants of the past, without faces or voices, yet driven by an insatiable hunger to drain the last trace of light from every living soul. Ten of them. Too close. Too fast. Rowena stepped backward, one careful step at a time, while the ten specters slowly circled around her. Their faceless heads tilted unnaturally, as though studying her every movement, every pulse of magic within her. The undead stretched their arms toward her. Their fingers, long and thin like needles, reached greedily for her life force. Rowena clenched the amulet with the white crystal in her trembling fingers, its surface cold as ice, meant to strengthen her. But her breath caught in her throat, her heart pounded wildly in her chest, and a desperate thought flashed through her mind: I can’t do this… She knew there were too many of them. Too fast. Too deadly. Suddenly, one of the specters lunged forward. Its translucent, decaying body shot through the air with terrifying speed, its claws reaching for her throat. Then, in that very instant, something unexpected happened. The world exploded in blinding light. A massive pillar of fire erupted upward, illuminating the half-collapsed ceiling of the temple. Flames swallowed the specter instantly. Its transparent body twisted violently within the burning radiance before slowly disintegrating into nothingness. A scream escaped from it — only to vanish beneath the roaring inferno. The remaining nine specters attacked at once, but fire surged around Rowena, forming an impenetrable circle. Sparks rained onto the stone floor, casting flickering light into the temple’s darkest corners while the specters hissed violently and recoiled from the element consuming their undead forms. The air filled with the stench of burning decay. Heat spread through the temple, making the ruins suddenly feel alive beneath the blaze. Sparks danced around Rowena, illuminating the tension carved across her face. As the smoke slowly began to clear, another figure appeared within the glow of the flames, standing atop a stone pedestal. A woman with red, wavy hair falling to her shoulder blades. Her eyes were the same deep crimson — piercing and unwavering. Her lightly tanned skin, kissed by the sun, only sharpened an expression that seemed both seductive and dangerous at once; beauty that was not merely decoration, but a weapon. She wore red oriental robes — light and airy, woven from delicate fabric that moved like hot desert wind across dunes. Loose where they offered coolness, fitted where beauty was meant to be seen, with elegant folds and slits that allowed effortless movement. Fine golden patterns flowed across the fabric like rolling waves. At first glance, it was obvious those robes did not come from the cold northern lands, but from Vienta — a port city resting at the edge of the Monolith Wasteland desert. A city where the sun never fully sets and hot winds dance through streets carved from sandstone. Vienta was a city of heat — not only because of its location, but because of what burned within its people: fire magic. Flame was not seen as something dangerous, but as a blessing. Fire was a symbol of life, movement, and transformation. It was mastered from childhood, not as a weapon, but as a living force capable of protecting, creating, and driving the world forward. Their magic was as beautiful as it was destructive. The city’s greatest temple — the Sanctuary of the Crimson Breath — was devoted to fire as a force of purification. There, people learned not only spells, but also discipline and philosophy: that fire, like emotion, must be guided by a steady hand, or it would consume everything. The garments worn by Vientan mages were more than clothing — they represented the balance between strength and beauty, between chaos and calm, between destructive power and the will to control it. And she embodied that balance perfectly. Her stance was firm and confident, her gaze sharp and unyielding. Every movement radiated strength and charisma that was impossible to ignore. She was the kind of woman who did not merely attract trouble — she faced it willingly, with fire in her heart and passion in her soul. “That wasn’t exactly elegant, was it?” a teasing voice said lightly. Rowena stood frozen, her breathing still tight and uneven. Her body remembered the danger; her eyes had not yet left the place where the specters had stood moments before. And yet… something had changed. That voice, that heat, that radiant figure had shattered the suffocating shadow of fear pressing down on her. She felt her shoulders loosen ever so slightly. Felt her heartbeat beginning to slow. Then she noticed it. More specters lurked within the shadows, but they had retreated deeper into the darkness of the temple. Their twisted silhouettes pulsed faintly, as though waiting for the perfect moment to strike again. The hateful energy that had soaked the walls only moments earlier suddenly seemed to falter. The mere presence of the red-haired woman forced them to hesitate. The woman with crimson hair smiled, shrugged casually, and then leapt gracefully down from the stone pedestal. She landed with such effortless lightness it seemed she had not touched solid ground at all, but moved instead to the rhythm of her own magic. With her staff in hand and heat still dancing around her, she walked toward Rowena without the slightest trace of fear. “Who… who are you?” Rowena breathed, her voice quieter than she intended. Thoughts swirled through her mind, but one rose above all the others: I’m not alone. “Aaliyah. Professional trouble enthusiast. And you’re… the one who almost died,” the woman with fiery hair replied with a grin, her staff still smoldering with lingering magic. Rowena swallowed. Her throat felt dry, yet the corners of her lips lifted faintly. “Rowena,” she answered softly. Then added, “Thank you… If it weren’t for you…” “You’d be dead, obviously,” Aaliyah agreed with a nod. “But I’m here, so you got lucky and survived. And by the way…” She walked closer, twirling her staff lazily around herself. “This temple’s got plenty more ghosts crawling around. Want to look through the place together? Just… temporarily.” “All right. But I should warn you — I usually cause more harm than help.” Aaliyah flashed a crooked grin and winked. “Perfect. Finally I won’t be the only disaster in the room.” Then she leaned in slightly and whispered, “But if something explodes, I’m blaming you. Rule number one.” They wandered through the temple long after the last flames had faded. The stone corridors were cold and silent, disturbed only now and then by the whispers of specters hidden in the darkness. Aaliyah carried her staff lightly before her, its red-orange glow licking across cracks in the walls, revealing shattered altars buried beneath dust and broken statues split apart by time. Eventually, they reached the main hall. Half the ceiling had collapsed, and pale light seeped through the opening above, blending with the smoldering glow of Aaliyah’s staff. The air felt heavy and exhausted — just like them. Without speaking, they began to search. Rowena moved methodically through the room, checking alcoves, pushing aside loose stones, and testing hidden compartments concealed beneath the pedestals of statues. If anything had survived, it would have been hidden away, protected from time and moisture alike. She opened one hidden compartment. Inside was nothing but dust and a few blackened fragments of wood. “Nothing here,” she muttered. Meanwhile, Aaliyah tapped her staff against one of the walls. A hollow sound echoed back. For a brief moment, excitement flashed in her eyes. She quickly shoved aside a stone slab and peered inside. Empty. Aaliyah exhaled sharply through her nose and closed the compartment again. “Magnificent adventure. Zero loot. Classic.” For a moment, she stared at the empty hiding place. “If there was ever anything here,” she added dryly, “someone faster and smarter than us already took it.” Rowena looked around the hall one last time, slowly and carefully, as though searching for some final overlooked possibility. Then she shook her head. “There’s nothing left,” she said quietly. “Only empty hiding places.” Silence settled between them for a moment. Not uncomfortable — shared. Aaliyah sat down on a broken step and pulled a small piece of dried meat from her satchel. Without much ceremony, she handed it to Rowena. “So,” she said with a faint smirk, “we searched the temple of a dead god… and the only things we found were dust and a terrible mood.” Rowena accepted the food, and the corners of her lips lifted slightly. They sat there together for a while amid the ruins of a temple that no longer had anything left to offer. And yet, the time did not feel wasted. Eventually, Aaliyah rose to her feet, brushed the dust from her robes, and looked toward the exit where a path disappeared between the broken stones into the unknown. “So what now?” she asked, turning her head toward Rowena. “Separate ways… or two disasters together?” Rowena stood slowly and nodded. “Together,” she answered simply. Aaliyah smiled — this time without mockery. Just quietly satisfied. “Good,” she said. “Then let’s go find trouble somewhere else.” And so their journey began — among the ruins of a shattered temple, between the whispers of the dead and the glow of fire.
