Chapter VI. – Cracks in the Armor

The desert was silent once more. But it was not a peaceful silence — it was hollow, heavy, stretched tight like a string moments before snapping. The creature that had risen from the depths now lay lifeless in the dust. Where bones once remained, there was only a glittering mosaic scattered beneath the night sky like a shattered dream. Aaliyah stood there breathing hard. Her shoulders trembled with every breath. The air around her still carried the fading traces of magic — fiery, hissing, corrosive. Her final spell had been lethal. It left behind nothing but silence and scorched earth. James stood a short distance away, silent, spear still in hand. The blade was coated in silvery ash, yet he did not move. As though expecting the creature to rise again. As though this victory itself were merely an illusion. Dust and sweat covered his body, but the expression on his face remained empty. Rowena stood beside Aaliyah, her palms pressed gently against her wounds. Her spells flowed like soft strands of light, slipping into the bleeding cuts and sealing them before scars could form. Healing magic pulsed between them, rhythmic and calm — like a mother’s touch. Her hands trembled, not from exhaustion, but because of what she had seen in the creature’s eyes moments before it fell apart. There had not been emptiness there. There had been pain. Rowena closed her eyes. “She… was suffering,” she whispered, barely audible. Aaliyah inhaled softly. The fire around her quieted, drawing closer to her body as though listening. “That doesn’t change anything,” she replied after a moment, her voice hardening. “It would’ve killed us.” “I know,” Rowena nodded. The light between her hands brightened briefly before slowly calming again. Aaliyah turned toward James. “That thing you were tracking,” she said, her voice sharper now, “before you found us… was it this?” “Yes. It attacked a caravan not far from here,” James answered calmly. He crouched closer, one hand resting on his knee, his eyes calm but focused. “They hired me,” he began quietly, “when the caravan was delayed for several days and no message came back. They feared something had happened, so I went looking for them.” For a moment, he lowered his gaze, as though reconstructing the memory piece by piece inside his mind. “First I found wagon tracks. Then scattered cargo. The canvas coverings were torn apart, wood splintered… and there was blood everywhere.” He paused briefly. “At first I thought it was one of the desert beasts.” A short breath escaped him. “Then I found the bodies. The bite marks on them didn’t match any creature that lives in this desert.” He raised his eyes. “There were too many teeth in that mouth,” he said slowly. “Packed together, crooked, uneven. Like they weren’t supposed to be there.” He reached into a pocket at his side. For a brief moment, he hesitated — then pulled out a tooth. It was long and curved, clearly belonging to a Sand Lizard at first glance. And yet… not entirely. Its surface was twisted, deformed, veined with fine black cracks. James straightened and rose to his feet. His movements were calm but decisive. Without a word, he handed the tooth to Aaliyah. She took it and examined it only briefly. “You’re right. Sand Lizards don’t have teeth like this,” she said calmly, though with certainty. “Their jaws are simple. This is… wrong.” Without hesitation, she passed the tooth to Rowena. The moment it touched her palm, she flinched slightly. The light surrounding her trembled and thickened, as though pressing against something unwilling to yield. She closed her eyes and focused entirely on the sensation for a moment. “I can feel dark magic coming from it,” she said quietly. “It’s old. Very old.” She opened her eyes again, concern visible within them. “I’ve never felt anything like this before.” James nodded slowly. “That’s the same feeling I had at the caravan,” he replied quietly. Aaliyah crossed her arms and stared into the darkness of the desert for a while. “It’s not wise to travel at night. We’ll wait until morning,” she said firmly. Then her expression hardened. “But let’s make one thing clear.” The flames crackled softly, as though reacting to the shift in her tone. “This isn’t just some stray beast. If ancient dark magic is involved… if someone awakened something like this or created it…” She paused briefly. “We should report it.” “In Vienta?” Rowena asked. “Yes. Or at the nearest oasis,” Aaliyah nodded. “Caravans pass through here constantly. And this thing wasn’t killing out of hunger.” James remained silent for a moment before slowly nodding. “I agree. First, we follow the trail and see where it leads. And if there’s more than one creature…” “…then this should be reported to Stonegard Castle,” Rowena finished quietly. The silence that followed was no longer filled only with fear. It carried resolve. Morning would not bring only light — it would bring direction. James straightened and calmly looked at both women. “Get some sleep,” he said quietly. “I’ll take the first watch. If anything moves, I’ll wake you.” Aaliyah shot him a brief look, as though considering an argument, but eventually only snorted in annoyance. “Fine. But if you let some ancient horror eat you alive, I’m holding it against you even after death.” Rowena nodded, still lost in thought. “Wake us at the first sign of danger,” she said quietly. “Whether it’s a sound… or just a feeling.” James gave a small nod. “A shadow in the sand is enough.” The women went to rest while he remained seated beside the fire. The night was cold, and the desert unusually silent. James placed his spear beside him on the ground, eyes fixed on the darkness — watchful, focused, ready for anything that might move around them. Morning was approaching, though the sun had not yet risen, and the desert still lay buried in darkness. James sat by the fire sharpening his dagger. His movements were precise, rhythmic, almost unnaturally calm. The fingers of his left hand were wrapped in bandages stained in places with dried blood. His breathing remained shallow and controlled. Rowena woke first. Quietly. Almost imperceptibly. She sat up, glanced around briefly, and her gaze immediately settled on the bandaged fingers on James’s hand. She said nothing. Instead, she reached for her satchel and stood. “I’ll try to find some herbs,” she whispered softly. Then she disappeared into the dim gray shadows. A short while later, Aaliyah awoke. She drew in a deep breath of the cold morning air, her eyes sharpening instantly — no trace of sleepiness, only immediate alertness. She noticed Rowena’s empty place first, then the bandages wrapped around James’s hand. Without speaking, she moved closer and sat across from him near the flames. Silently, she watched him. Her gaze sharp. Attentive. “You’re pretending to be invincible again,” she said quietly. “I don’t want anyone pitying me,” he replied without looking up. “No one pities you,” she answered. “But maybe… someone’s simply afraid you’ve stopped caring whether you live or die.” He looked at her then. And this time, the look lingered. “And what about you, Aaliyah?” he asked quietly. “Do you still care about… anything?” She wanted to answer immediately. Something fiery, quick, clever. But instead, she lowered her eyes. “I don’t know,” she admitted softly. James rose to his feet, slowly walked around the fire, and sat beside her. Then he placed a hand gently on her shoulder. Not firmly. Not confidently. Almost… as though he were doing it for the first time. Rowena stood a short distance away. The wind tangled her long black hair, and the skirt of her dark blue robes whispered softly around her ankles, yet she did not move. Healing herbs rested in her hands, though she had already forgotten why she had gone to gather them. Before her, beside the fire, sat Aaliyah — and James beside her. She watched their eyes meet. She saw the way Aaliyah smiled in that slightly uncertain manner of hers, the kind she used whenever she tried to soften the silence around her. And she saw that James — a man who often seemed to speak more with his eyes than words — understood that smile completely. Rowena sighed softly. There was no jealousy in it. Only concern. Aaliyah was like fire — radiant, warmhearted, untamed. But fire was fragile. It needed air, and if it received too little… it died. And James? Rowena observed him carefully. He was not the kind of man who opened himself easily. His calmness was deceptive — the silence of someone who carried pain in his bones and had grown accustomed to solitude simply to avoid losing anyone else. What if he doesn’t hurt her on purpose? she thought. What if he won’t even realize he hurt her? Aaliyah had a habit of giving every part of herself away. And Rowena knew she rarely kept anything for herself afterward. She feared not the man sitting beside her friend, but what Aaliyah would willingly sacrifice just to keep him from leaving. Her gaze lowered to the plants in her hands. She understood wounds that bled. But the wounds inside the heart? Those healed far more slowly. And if that pain ever came, it would not be James gathering the shattered pieces. It would be her. Rowena. And that was alright. She would do it again and again if necessary. Because Aaliyah was her friend. Not an ideal. Not a dream. A living person who had spent so much of her life helping others that she finally deserved someone watching over her for once. Rowena placed the herbs back into her satchel and walked toward the fire. Both of them looked up at her. “You should eat something,” she said calmly. “Things are only going to get worse from here.” Aaliyah smiled at her. And Rowena smiled back.
