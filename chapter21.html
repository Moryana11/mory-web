Chapter XXI – The crossing

The path ahead led across uneven terrain, where the sand gradually blended with jagged boulders and ancient rocks, their surfaces covered in faded carvings. The sun climbed higher, and the sky took on an unnaturally pale shade. Commander DaVinci, riding at the head of the expedition, raised his hand. The column halted. “There used to be a watchtower here. The ruins are directly across from the gorge. We should be careful.” James moved closer. “Expecting an ambush?” “No. But I can feel that something remained behind. Not from humans.” His fingers tightened around the reins. Rowena narrowed her eyes. She dismounted and pulled the orb from her satchel. Its glow spread across the stones like ripples on water. “There’s residual magic here,” she said calmly. “It isn’t alive. But… it feels as though something powerful passed through this place and left traces behind. Invisible… almost.” Meanwhile, Aaliyah drew her staff, its flames burning quietly. “We’re close to the epicenter. This place knows pain.” The knights spread into a defensive formation. Their horses neighed nervously, and one young rider had to cling to his saddle when his mount suddenly recoiled. “We’ll take a break,” Commander DaVinci decided. “But we stay armored.” The three of them dismounted and wandered for a moment among the stones, cracked by heat and time alike. Rowena placed her hand against a rock that pulsed beneath her palm with the faint echo of ancient suffering. Her vision darkened for an instant — a flash. A vision. The dark knight, Zarveth, kneeling in the dust and whispering unknown words into the cracked earth. Then a scream… not human. “You saw something?” Aaliyah asked, noticing Rowena stagger slightly. “Yes… perhaps remnants of a ritual.” At that moment, the wind rose. Not violently, but insistently. It slithered between the rocks, hissed through their cracks, and carried with it the stench of decay. The horses reacted immediately. Several reared up, others began backing away nervously and neighing as though trying to escape an invisible threat. One of the knights barely managed to hold onto the reins when his horse tried to break free. DaVinci sharpened his gaze. “Hold them!” he commanded. The animals sensed something human eyes still could not see. And then it happened. A short distance away, between the boulders near the edge of the gorge, something moved. Not suddenly. Slowly. Heavily. A figure emerged from the shadows of the rocks, step by step, as though something compelled it to keep walking even though it had long since lost any reason to do so. An undead. Fragments of armor and a red cloak hung from its shoulders. Its skin was dried and cracked, missing entirely in places and exposing bone beneath. Its head tilted strangely to one side, empty eye sockets staring through the world — not at them, but somewhere far beyond. It walked. Simply walked. The sand whispered softly beneath its feet, each step sounding like fingernails scraping against stone. James instinctively reached for his daggers, but DaVinci gestured for him to wait. “Not yet,” he whispered. Rowena felt a chill run down her spine. The orb at her side remained dark, yet the air around the undead was saturated with the same corrupted echo she had sensed in her vision. Aaliyah tightened her grip on her staff. “It’s not alone,” she said quietly. The undead slowly disappeared behind a rocky outcrop. For a moment, silence fell. Only the wind hissed once more between the stones while the horses stamped nervously against the ground. DaVinci scanned the expedition. His gaze was hard, resolute. Then he dismounted, his boots landing firmly against the earth. “This is where we split up,” he said quietly, but loud enough for everyone to hear. He pointed toward the horses. “You stay here. Keep the animals together and be ready to move. The rest of you come with me.” Several knights nodded and took hold of the reins while the others dismounted alongside him. DaVinci turned toward the trio. “You’re coming with us.” James nodded silently and drew his daggers. Aaliyah shifted her staff into her other hand. Rowena pulled her cloak tighter around herself, eyes fixed on the place where the undead had vanished. “We move quietly,” DaVinci continued. “I want to know where it’s going. It won’t be alone. It’s wearing the cloak and armor of the knights of Stonegard Castle. They never travel the desert in groups smaller than three.” He gave a brief hand signal. The group moved forward, their footsteps muffled by sand and the shadows of the rocks. Behind them remained the horses and part of the knights, ready to intervene at any moment. The gorge swallowed them whole. The air grew colder. The sounds of the desert faded away. The walls rose around them like dark ramparts, and the sunlight between them narrowed into thin, cold streaks. Ahead of them, it appeared again. The undead. It walked slowly, with a strange certainty, as though it knew the path better than the living did. Suddenly, it vanished behind a rocky outcrop. DaVinci raised his hand. They stopped. A muffled scraping sound echoed out. Then another. As they cautiously moved around the stone, a smaller rocky chamber opened before them — a natural hollow whose floor was strewn with bones and shattered remnants of armor. At its center stood a stone. A tall, uneven monolith, completely stained with dried blood that ran down its sides in dark, sticky streaks. The sand around it had hardened and blackened, as though blood had seeped into it long ago, again and again. Around the stone stood undead knights. They did not advance. They did not attack. They simply stood in a circle, armor ruined, cloaks torn, heads lowered. Their bodies swayed slowly from side to side in a rhythm that could not be heard, only felt — like the beating of a heart that should have stopped long ago. The air was heavy. Suffocating. Saturated with old blood and corrupted magic. Rowena felt her stomach tighten. The echo of pain she had touched within the rock was stronger here, concentrated. “They’re bound to that stone,” she said quietly. “It’s holding them here. Like witnesses… or sacrifices.” DaVinci tightened his grip around the hilt of his sword and stepped forward. Determination glinted in his eyes. “Destroy them,” he whispered. But James instantly grabbed his arm. “No.” The commander turned toward him, surprised by the tone in his voice. “Why not?” James scanned the area warily. “We need to leave. If this is where it began… then it’s possible he’ll want to return here.” DaVinci frowned. The lines across his forehead deepened, and for a brief moment his gaze grew heavy. “If we kill them now,” James continued in a low voice, his eyes still fixed on the circle of undead, “Zarveth will realize we found this place. He’ll relocate and start again. With different bodies. Different victims. And next time, he’ll cover his tracks. But if we leave, he’ll have no reason to change anything. He’ll come back here. And meanwhile, we gain time. We prepare. We plan a killing blow.” For a moment, the only sound was the faint swaying of the bodies. DaVinci stood motionless. He stared at the monolith for a long time, as though searching it for an answer. “So we let him believe he’s safe.” “Yes,” James confirmed. “And when we strike, we destroy him.” The commander clenched his jaw, though his voice remained calm. “Very well.” He gave a quiet signal to retreat. “Remember this place. We’ll return.” The group began withdrawing along the same path they had taken in. Their steps were slow and careful, not a single unnecessary sound made. No one looked back, yet all of them could feel the gaze of the bloodstained monolith against their backs. When they finally left the gorge, the air grew warm again and the light widened around them. The horses greeted them with restless stamping and subdued neighs, as though they too felt relief. DaVinci mounted his horse last. He looked back one final time toward the dark cliffs. “Stonegard Castle is still a day away. We move faster.” The expedition set out once more. Beyond the gorge, the first stone outcroppings of the castle had already become visible. Its towers were low, but strong. The fortress was not beautiful, but unyielding. Built not for comfort, but for survival. And right now — it was their last hope.
