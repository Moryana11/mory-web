Chapter XXX. – In the house of light

The chambers in the eastern wing of the castle were reserved for guests who needed peace and quiet. Brienne was given one of the larger rooms — an old chamber overlooking the gardens, where the muted sound of a fountain and the chirping of sparrows among the olive trees drifted softly through the windows. While the servants helped remove her torn clothing and washed the scratches from her legs, Brienne remained silent. She gazed out the window quietly, almost absent-mindedly. But inside… inside, she was counting steps. Measuring distances. Listening to voices, sensing the magic flowing through the corridors, memorizing the movements of the guards. They gave her clean linen clothes, warm broth, and fresh bread with a crisp crust. One of the younger servants offered to assist her with the bath, but Brienne merely shook her head and lowered her gaze once more. “Thank you,” she murmured softly. “I can manage alone.” The servant touched her shoulder gently. “We’ll leave you to rest for a while, my lady. If you need anything, just ring the bell.” Brienne nodded. And the moment she was finally alone, her eyes darkened. She rose. Every movement became light, precise, confident. Crossing the room, she approached the fireplace where flames crackled softly behind the iron grate. She rested her fingers against the stone above it — ancient stone, soaked with smoke and old magic. Placing her palm flat against it, she whispered words in the ancient language of the Shadows. Images flooded her mind instantly: guards inside the armory. The seal vault. Commander Hander’s chambers. A hidden room lined with silver wards. Parsons inside the sanctuary beyond the library. The fortress breathed with magic — pure, radiant… and vulnerable. To the south, towering cliffs protected the castle, with the sea stretching endlessly beneath them, while on every other side lay only hostile desert. And yet neither stone, nor water, nor sand could stop the darkness. Because it was already inside. And its name was Brienne. The following day, Parsons came to visit her. In her hand she carried a silver amulet — simple, smooth, shaped like a teardrop. “Evalyne wore this,” Parsons said gently. “We found it beside her body. It belongs to you now.” Brienne held her breath. Tears filled her eyes instantly. With trembling fingers she accepted the amulet — and then suddenly collapsed into Parsons’s embrace. She shook with grief so convincingly, so perfectly, that even the archmage’s own eyes grew moist. “You have a pure heart, child,” Parsons whispered softly. “Not anymore,” Brienne murmured faintly, turning her gaze away. Two days later, she moved far more freely throughout the castle. An elderly healer in the communal dining hall grew fond of her and often prepared herbal tea for her personally. A castle scribe brought her books of old legends after discovering her love for reading. Several knights invited her into the training hall to show her how the young recruits practiced. Brienne smiled politely. Sometimes she even laughed. But always softly. Humbly. Exactly as everyone expected her to. To the others, she became a ray of light after the storm — proof that even after massacre and horror, something innocent could still survive. And she played her role perfectly.
