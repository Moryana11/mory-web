Chapter VIII. – The key between times

The temple was changing in silence. The walls, once bare and crumbling, now carried impressions. Not paintings — memories. They could only be understood by those willing to look backward. Hands reaching into emptiness. Faces twisted in screams. Fingers clutching at what had already been lost. And between all of it — mist. Not ordinary moisture, but a fog woven from thoughts themselves. Heavy. Sweet. Familiar and yet foreign. It drifted downward through cracks in the ceiling as though seeping from another layer of existence. It carried the taste of the past and the scent of guilt. Every breath burned like confession. Rowena stood silently. Her eyes remained fixed on one of the walls where a scene slowly began to emerge: a young girl running through a dense forest, breath ragged, heart pounding in terror. Rowena recognized the sight instantly. She knew it was her. And she knew what would come next. The image was not merely an image — it was a return. Painful. Inevitable. When Aaliyah appeared beside her, there were no footsteps. Only presence. As though she herself were becoming another shadow. “You know what we have to do, don’t you?” Aaliyah finally broke the silence. “I can see it in your eyes.” Rowena nodded slowly. “Yes. We have to go through.” “Through the image? Those shadows on the walls?” Aaliyah asked, her gaze darting uneasily across the flickering silhouettes. “They’re not just shadows,” Rowena whispered, extending her hand toward one of them. Her fingers brushed the wall for only a moment, and the shadow trembled beneath her touch. “They’re memories. Fragments left behind… or pulled out of us. Every piece is a key.” Aaliyah shivered, her eyes wandering across the surrounding visions. Her face paled when she caught sight of her own past. “This isn’t remembering,” she whispered. “This is… real.” “Memories here aren’t dead,” Rowena replied quietly. “They’re alive. And they’re keeping us trapped.” “How…” Aaliyah asked, her voice faltering. “How do you even know what this is?” Rowena slowly tore her gaze away from the vision on the wall and looked at her. “I found a record,” Rowena said quietly. “Not in a book. Not on a scroll. In a sealed chest beneath the Archive, deep below the main hall. It was a thought preserved inside a magical orb. A trace left behind by someone who had studied Fragments of Time for years… and never returned from one.” Her voice lowered even further. “He told me memories are not the past. They are keys. And if we close our eyes to them, the temple will never let us leave.” Aaliyah stepped closer to the wall. She touched it. At once, a vision materialized across the stone like something from an ancient dream — or a nightmare no one wished to witness. A graveyard appeared. Many figures stood there, blurred and indistinct like shadows buried within the mist of time. Some were almost invisible. Others emerged with painful clarity. At the center stood a man in a dark cloak, tall and rigid — Lord Hobs Kastler. His face was stern, yet filled with quiet grief. In his hands he carried an urn. A simple vessel. And yet it seemed to contain all the sorrow in the world. The urn belonged to his son, Henry — the boy who had sacrificed himself. Henry, who during the attack of the dark wolves had not hesitated. He faced the danger alone to save the other children. His courage had cost him his life… but it had also left behind hope that survived after him. Near Lord Hobs Kastler, barely visible among the shadows, stood a small girl. Rowena. Still a child. Her face was pale, almost translucent, her eyes hollow and filled with pain. She stood apart from the others, as though wanting both to exist there and disappear entirely. She had been one of the children Henry saved. One of those who survived — but whose soul remained forever marked by loss. Her gaze felt distant, as though she were watching someone else’s memory. And yet it was a truth she could never escape. The entire vision pulsed with sorrow. It screamed silently, demanding acceptance. This was not mere remembrance. It was surrendering to truth. A truth many refused to acknowledge. “This…” Aaliyah whispered. “This is you?” “Yes,” Rowena nodded slowly. “If you refuse to face your memories, if you continue denying them, you’ll remain trapped in this temple forever. You won’t move forward. Because this place feeds on denial.” At that moment, the room fell silent. And the silence became something heavy. Suffocating. It was not simply the air growing colder. The chill reached beneath the skin itself. A pressure tightening around the chest and slowing every breath. As though the walls themselves were holding back a scream. The shadows began to tremble. They shifted, thickened, gained weight. From vague outlines, reality itself began to emerge. They were no longer mere silhouettes — they became traces of actions, records of faces, echoes of words once spoken that still carried the burden of choice. Every movement, every pulse of darkness, seemed to remind them of the price of the past. The air within the temple grew heavier. And with it came the terrible understanding that time here did not move forward — it circled endlessly. Every unspoken moment returned sharpened like a blade. “We have to do this now,” Rowena said. “Before the keys fall apart.” Aaliyah swallowed hard. “And what if… we get lost?” “Then we’ll find each other again,” Rowena answered quietly. “But first, each of us has to walk alone.” Silence. Then footsteps. One. Then another. Heavy. Determined. A figure emerged from the shadows. James had been standing apart the entire time, hidden within darkness, silently observing everything around him. His eyes followed the movement of the memories, the faces and stories carved across the walls, as though trying to understand every detail, every pain, every scream. Then slowly, he stepped forward. He entered one of the flickering shadows. His form immediately began dissolving, as though he himself were becoming part of the memory — part of the pain, part of the past. Just before vanishing completely, he turned slightly and whispered: “I’ll see you on the other side.” The temple trembled. The mist rose higher. Rowena drew in a breath.Then she stepped forward. The wall swallowed her whole. Without sound. Without trace. Only Aaliyah remained. And her own past. She took one step. And the temple erupted with light. Searching for the Key — Rowena’s Point of View Leaves. Air. Fear. Rowena ran. Her feet sank into the damp earth while branches scratched against her arms. Behind her ran the other children — four girls and three boys. All equally young. Equally lost. And behind them— the sound. Not barking. Something worse. Wolves howling. Moments later, they saw them. Darker than shadow itself, with glowing red eyes. “Keep moving!” shouted the boy at the front. Henry. The son of Lord Hobs Kastler. Fear lingered in his eyes, but so did determination. His stride remained steady. His short brown hair was disheveled by the wind, and the fine noble clothes he wore were already torn and covered in mud from running beside the others. At his waist hung a short sword. A gift for his twelfth birthday. The family crest was engraved into its hilt. “Run!” Henry shouted. Then suddenly, he stopped. With one smooth motion, he drew his sword. Not like a child. Like a warrior who had already accepted his role long ago. There was no fear in his eyes. “No!” Rowena screamed, but she already knew she could no longer stop him. Henry glanced back over his shoulder. A smile. Small. Calm. And his eyes said everything words could not. You have to live. The darkness shifted apart. More wolves emerged from the undergrowth — six, perhaps eight. Larger than ordinary wolves. Heavier. Every step they took left shadows behind them. Their teeth gleamed inside black jaws. Henry stepped forward. Left foot slightly ahead of the right, almost like a dance. He raised his sword. A growl echoed through the forest. Deep. Heavy. Like death itself breathing. The first wolf leapt. Henry struck. Elegant. Precise. His blade found its mark. The wolf howled and disappeared into the grass. But more were already charging behind it. Too many. Rowena kept running. Tears streamed down her face, but she refused to slow. Because she knew that if she turned around— something inside her would break forever. The scene changed. Rowena now stood in a graveyard, still trapped within her child’s body. Before her stood Henry. The boy who had saved her. He spoke softly, yet firmly. “You didn’t abandon me there. Stop blaming yourself.” Rowena burst into tears. “I should have stayed with you.” “No, you shouldn’t have,” Henry answered gently. “I wanted you to live. And I still do. I’m glad I could see you again… but it’s time for you to move forward.” Henry stepped closer and wrapped his arms around her. “This isn’t goodbye. I’ll still be watching over you.” Her body trembled. She did not want to leave him. Not again. But eventually, she stepped toward the doors of the Kastler family crypt. And suddenly, Rowena stood within the temple once more. But this time, it looked different. As though with every accepted shadow, the temple itself was weakening. And yet— it was far from over.
