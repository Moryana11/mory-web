Chapter XXV. – In the silence of the heart

Thomas had been restless all day. After the trio left with DaVinci to meet Commander Hander and High Mage Parsons, he had been forced to return to his watch. He stood upon the battlements, watching the courtyard, the gates, and the desert dunes while his thoughts continuously drifted elsewhere. To Rowena. He had seen her only for a moment. Exhausted, shaken… and yet one image had carved itself beneath his skin more deeply than he wanted to admit. That man. The way he held her up. The way he leaned too close to her. Thomas kept telling himself it had been necessary, that it meant nothing — but reason held far less power than the feeling tightening inside his chest. His watch ended only with the coming of evening. Once the castle sank into dim shadows and torchlight replaced the day, Thomas returned to his chambers. He removed his armor, bathed, and changed into clean clothes. Still, peace would not come. And so, after nightfall, Thomas quietly left his room and made his way down the corridor lined with stone walls that led toward Rowena’s chambers. Every step he took was firm, yet his heart beat faster than he expected. He knew this was not merely an ordinary visit. It was a moment he had waited months for. He stopped before her door. For a brief instant, he hesitated and glanced around to make sure the corridor was empty. Then he knocked softly, carefully. After a moment, the door opened. Rowena stood there dressed in a loose nightgown made of soft fabric. Thomas froze for a heartbeat. His gaze instinctively dropped lower — to the loose nightgown — before quickly returning to her face. “Sorry,” he breathed quietly, startled by his own reaction. Rowena seemed just as surprised by his arrival. Something flickered in her eyes between joy and embarrassment. The realization that she stood in the doorway wearing only a nightgown made her stiffen slightly. “I… I’ll just get my robe,” she said quickly, stepping back into the room. Without waiting, she turned and nearly hurried deeper inside. Thomas remained in the doorway for only a fraction of a second before following her. The moment he entered, he quietly but firmly shut the door behind him. He did not want anyone seeing them together in the corridor. Not tonight. Not like this. Meanwhile, Rowena slipped a robe over her shoulders and tied it more tightly around herself. When she turned back toward him, it was obvious she felt a little embarrassed… and yet equally glad that he was there. A brief silence settled between them. Close. Unfamiliar. And yet strangely intimate. “I…” Thomas began, then stopped himself. In the end, he only smiled faintly. “I think I came a little late.” Rowena shook her head. “No,” she answered softly. “Just… unexpectedly.” For a moment, Thomas simply looked at her. Not as though studying her — more with a quiet concern he could not hide. Even in the soft light of the lamps, it was obvious she was still pale and exhausted, her shoulders slightly slumped as though the weight of the past days had not yet left her. “You still look worn out,” he said quietly. “Did they take you to the infirmary?” Rowena nodded. “Yes. The healers said it’s mostly exhaustion. Nothing serious.” A faint smile touched her lips. “Healers don’t have much endurance.” The attempt at humor barely held together. Thomas tightened his jaw. “I was worried about you all day,” he admitted plainly. “I had to come.” The honesty lingered between them in the air. Rowena lowered her gaze, though not out of embarrassment this time. “I’m glad you’re here,” she said softly. For a while, they stood in silence. Then Thomas drew a slow breath, as though trying to redirect the conversation — or perhaps steady his thoughts. “That man,” he said carefully at last. “The one holding you earlier…” Rowena lifted her head. “James.” Thomas disliked the name already simply because of how calmly she spoke it. “Who is he?” he asked. Not sharply. More restrained than that. “And where did you meet him?” Rowena sat down beside the table and wrapped her hands around the cup of water left there from dinner. “He’s a mercenary,” she answered. “He traveled alone for a long time. We met on the road through the desert… and if he hadn’t been there, I might not still be alive.” She looked directly at Thomas. “He helped us. More than once.” Thomas nodded slowly. He accepted the information, even if he did not entirely like it. “So he is one of them,” he murmured, more to himself than to her. “One of them?” Rowena repeated, confused. “What do you mean?” Thomas hesitated briefly before answering calmly, without sharpness. “With mercenaries. People who fight for coin, not for an oath.” Rowena frowned. The words clearly displeased her. “That’s not fair,” she said quietly, but firmly. “Maybe he fights for money, but that doesn’t mean he has no honor.” She looked directly into Thomas’s eyes. “James could have left. More than once,” she continued. “No one would have blamed him. And yet he stayed. He risked his life. For us.” Her fingers tightened slightly around the cup. “Those aren’t the choices of someone who only cares about payment,” she added calmly. Thomas stiffened slightly, and something colder entered his posture — the quiet arrogance of a knight raised his entire life within a different order of the world. “You think better of them than they deserve,” he said calmly, though the softness had faded from his voice. “I know mercenaries. I’ve seen what they leave behind once the coins run out.” He paused, his gaze lingering on her a fraction longer than necessary. “The way you speak about him…” he added quietly. “It sounds like you care about him.” The words escaped before he could stop himself. “I care that he protected us,” she replied. “And that he never lied to us. That’s all.” Thomas lowered his head slightly, but irritation flashed within his eyes. “That’s more than I’d expect from someone who fights for money,” he said harshly. The words came from pride. From convictions hammered into him from the moment he first put on armor. Rowena drew a slow breath, as though trying to hold onto her composure. Her fingers tightened around the cup again, no longer from uncertainty alone, but from quiet resistance. “Maybe,” she said softly but firmly, “you just see the world too much in black and white.” She raised her eyes to him. There was no anger there. Only exhaustion and disappointment. “You see an oath and armor,” she continued. “I saw a man who always stood where he could protect us with his own life.” For a moment, she fell silent, as though carefully weighing her next words. “I don’t care who he is according to the orders,” she added calmly. “I care about who he was when lives were at stake.” Thomas straightened slightly. Pride mixed within him with something he refused to acknowledge. “And what if you’re wrong?” he asked. “What if one day he accepts a better offer?” “Then that will be his choice,” she answered without hesitation. “Just as it’s your choice to believe honor belongs only to those who wear the right colors.” The words struck him. For a moment, neither spoke. The only sound in the room was the faint crack of wood in the fireplace, still holding traces of warmth. “I’m trying to protect you,” he said at last, more quietly. “I don’t want you letting someone close who could betray you.” Rowena’s expression softened. “I know,” she answered. “And that’s exactly why I’m being honest with you.” She stepped closer and carefully touched his hand. Not firmly. Just with the tips of her fingers, as though testing whether the same warmth still existed between them. Thomas stiffened — only for an instant. Then he took her hand gently into both of his palms. Slowly, he lifted it to his lips and kissed the back of her hand. Softly. Respectfully. The way he had always expressed feelings he considered serious. He held her hand for another moment before slowly letting it go. “I trust your judgment,” he said calmly. And the words sounded exactly as they should — reasonable, composed, mature. “If you say the man is honorable… then I believe you.” Rowena looked at him with faint surprise. “Thank you,” she said quietly. Thomas nodded. “We won’t speak about him anymore,” he added. Outwardly, it sounded as though the matter had been settled. But inside… inside, it was different. Beneath the calm mask, his blood still burned. The image of James returned to him again and again — his closeness, the way he stood beside Rowena, the way he held her. Thomas could not truly trust her judgment on this. Not because he doubted her. But because he knew men without oaths far too well. Rowena noticed that the tension in his shoulders had not fully faded. “Are you alright?” she asked carefully. “Yes,” he answered immediately. And this time, it was only half a lie. He reached out and gently brushed a strand of hair away from her forehead — a small, quiet gesture of closeness. “I’m just glad you’re safe.” Rowena did not stop his hand. Quite the opposite — for a brief moment, she leaned lightly into the touch, as though wanting to rest against it. “I am,” she said softly. “Now I am.” Thomas leaned closer and gently pressed his forehead against hers. He closed his eyes. For a moment, everything else disappeared — the castle, the duties, the thoughts that had weighed on him all day. Only this fragile, quiet moment between them remained. Her warmth. Her breathing. Rowena did not move away. She only let out a soft breath, and her hand carefully touched his arm. “I’ll stay a little longer,” he murmured quietly. “If you don’t mind.” Rowena shook her head. “I don’t mind.” They remained standing like that for several seconds. Maybe longer. Time dissolved within the silence. Then Thomas slowly opened his eyes, though he did not pull his forehead away from hers. “I missed you,” he said softly. Rowena drew a faint breath. “I missed you too,” she answered just as quietly. Thomas let her words linger between them for another moment before slowly pulling away and taking a deep breath. “I should return to my chambers,” he said quietly. “I have watch duty in the morning.” Rowena nodded. She knew him too well. She knew he would not stay — he took his role as a knight deeply seriously. Duty, watch, order, responsibility… they were rooted deep inside him. They were the axis around which his entire world revolved. It saddened her. But she had long since accepted it. Rowena looked at him one more time. “Go,” she said softly. “So you’ll be rested in the morning.” Thomas nodded, grateful for how easy everything always felt with her. “Good night.” “Good night.” When the door quietly closed behind him, Rowena remained standing in the middle of the room. Alone. Slowly, she pulled the robe tighter around herself and took a deep breath. She walked to the window and gazed out into the night-covered courtyard. Torches burned steadily while the guards changed shifts in precise intervals. The world continued according to the order Thomas understood so well. She rested her forehead against the cool glass and closed her eyes for a moment. She did not cry. She simply allowed herself to feel the emptiness that always came whenever she chose to wait once again. “One day,” she whispered, barely audibly. “One day we’ll finally be together.” Then she stepped away from the window, extinguished the lamp, and let the room sink into shadow. Aaliyah could not sleep. Her mind was far too restless. The chamber was larger than she had expected. Too large. And far too quiet. The fire crackled softly in the fireplace, and the silk curtains kept out the night chill, yet Aaliyah still felt… foreign there. Alone. Not peacefully alone, but in a way that felt uncomfortable, almost suffocating. It was not the first time she had a bed to herself — but after so many months, she had forgotten what that kind of solitude felt like. She and Rowena had traveled together across the world, sharing tents, rooms, and silence beside campfires. She had grown used to feeling her presence nearby, hearing her breathing or whispered words just before sleep. But now Rowena was somewhere else. And Aaliyah was left alone with her thoughts. She tossed and turned, sat for a while, paced the room. At last, she draped a cloak over her shoulders, slipped into soft shoes, and quietly opened the door. At night, the stone corridors of Stonegard felt different. As though the castle breathed in another rhythm — slower, yet restless. Her footsteps sounded louder, the shadows stretched longer, and the silence itself felt heavier. She had no destination. She simply needed movement. Space. Air. And there, in one of the dim corridors beyond a corner touched only by pale moonlight she ran into him. William.
