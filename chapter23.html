Chapter XXIII. – A memory in the night

Everyone else was asleep. Only Rowena remained seated beside the dying fire, where the warmth of the embers slowly faded just like her thoughts. The wind quietly toyed with the edges of her cloak while her eyes stared into the darkness. Beyond the dunes, the silhouette of Stonegard Castle rose against the horizon — black and sharp. She rested her chin against her knees, and one name echoed through her mind. Thomas. Her breath caught. How long had it been since she had last seen him? Months. Far too many months. And now… now they would finally meet again. The thought warmed her chest while tightening her stomach at the same time. She was excited — and equally terrified. Joy mixed with uncertainty that slipped beneath her skin like cold water. What if his feelings had changed? What if hers were the only ones that had endured? Their relationship had never been simple. She was a healer from the Tower of Pure Light, bound by an oath to care only for the well-being of others, never her own. And he… a knight for whom family was a burden. A weakness an enemy could exploit. And yet, somehow, they had found each other. Despite orders. Despite vows. She remembered their first meeting. It had been in the Tower of Pure Light, inside an infirmary bathed in the soft glow of lamps and the scent of herbs drifting through the air. An entire unit had been brought in after a raid in the Abyss of Syleus. At the time, Rowena had been kneeling beside a wounded man, her hands stained with blood mixed with healing salve, her mind focused solely on her work. She did not look up until she heard the voice of the head healer above her. “Althea will take over for him,” she said briefly. “Come with me. We have severe cases in the northern wing.” Without hesitation, Rowena stepped aside, quickly wiping her hands against a cloth before following her into another section of the infirmary. There was no peace there. The air felt heavier, saturated with pain, muffled cries, and the metallic scent of blood. And that was where she saw him. He stood nearby, tall and broad-shouldered in scratched armor. His dark red hair was tied back into a braid, the sides of his head shaved clean. Tension reflected in his brown eyes. He was holding another knight in his arms. Badly wounded. The man hung limp against him, his head falling helplessly toward his chest while blood streamed down his armor and dripped onto the stone floor. The knight held him firmly, yet carefully — as though afraid that a single wrong movement might hurt him even more. Then their eyes met. He froze. Only slightly. But Rowena noticed it. His breath caught for the briefest moment. “Please,” he said quietly, never taking his eyes off her. “Help him.” Rowena nodded before she even realized it herself. “Lay him here,” she said, motioning toward an empty bed. Her voice was calm, steady — the voice of someone who knew exactly what she was doing. The knight obeyed immediately. He knelt and carefully lowered the wounded man onto the cot. As he loosened his grip, his hands trembled for an instant. Rowena noticed that too. Just as she noticed the blood on his forearm, the small cuts across his knuckles, the tear in his glove. “You’re injured,” she said softly. “You should have someone tend to you as well.” For a moment, the knight glanced at his own hand before lifting his eyes back to her. “It’s nothing,” he answered calmly. “Just scratches.” Rowena inhaled quietly, but said nothing more as she bent over the wounded man. She loosened the bloodstained straps, pushed aside the remains of the armor, and began to work. Her fingers brushed lightly against torn flesh, and a soft pearlescent light spread between them. The wounds slowly began to close beneath her touch, and the bleeding eased. Thomas remained standing nearby. He did not speak. He only watched. When the wounded man suddenly gasped and jerked in pain, Thomas stepped closer without being asked and placed a hand firmly on his shoulder. The touch was not merely support for the injured knight — it felt as though he were holding the entire moment together. It affected Rowena far more than she expected. The pearlescent light between her fingers wavered for a brief instant. Her magic reacted to her unrest, intensifying, losing its smooth control. She quickly took a breath and forced herself to focus, yet despite herself, she looked up. Their eyes met. There was a calmness in his gaze that disarmed her completely. She looked away and finished the healing with one final, silent sweep of light. When she lowered her hands, she realized her fingers were trembling slightly. That startled her more than anything else. That had never happened before. She did not flinch. She did not pull away. And he noticed — that stillness, that silent permission. Slowly, he released one of her hands, as though giving her time to retreat at any moment, and guided it upward. When his fingers touched her face, the contact was almost imperceptible. Warmth spread across her skin, and for a moment Rowena felt as though the entire world had stopped. His touch was firm and gentle at once — nothing like the rough hand of a warrior she would have expected. His thumb rested against her cheekbone for a brief moment, lingering there. Then it moved. Lightly. Slowly. His thumb brushed softly across her cheek in a movement so faint it carried neither urgency nor demand. For a moment longer, he remained there in that slow, nearly invisible motion, as though committing the moment to memory. Then his thumb stilled, and he leaned slightly closer to her, only enough for her to hear him. “You’re beautiful,” he said quietly. The words struck her deeply. For a moment, she could not answer. She simply stood there with his hand against her face, her breath caught somewhere between inhaling and exhaling. Her own hand moved slightly, instinctively touching his wrist. Not to push him away. More to make certain he was real. “You shouldn’t say things like that,” she breathed softly. Her voice was barely audible, strained and fragile all at once. Her fingers still rested lightly against his wrist, as though that touch were the only thing anchoring her to the present moment. For a second, nothing happened. Then something shifted in his eyes. “I know,” he answered quietly. “But I can’t help it.” She knew she should step away now. At the same time, she realized how difficult that was. And in that exact moment, footsteps echoed through the room. Reality struck Rowena sharply. The infirmary doors opened, and another healer entered, absorbed in her own thoughts, carrying a bowl of clean bandages in her hands. She did not look toward them, heading straight for the table on the opposite side of the room. Rowena’s heart pounded so hard she could feel it in her throat. Thomas was still too close. His hand had not fully left her face, and the way they stood together was far too intimate, far too different from what anyone should see. Slowly, almost imperceptibly, she stepped away from him. Her fingers slipped from his wrist as though nothing had happened. “The wounds are healed,” she said, this time more firmly. Professionally. Without a tremor. Thomas drew a single deep breath. Then he carefully stepped closer again, only by a small distance, leaning down just enough for his voice to become barely audible. “Come tonight,” he whispered calmly. “To the woods by the lake. After dark.” His gaze was open, free of pressure. “If you don’t want to, I’ll understand. I won’t come looking for you. I won’t tell anyone.” He stepped back to give her space. “I just…” he added quietly, “would like to speak with you. Without witnesses.” From that moment on, they met in secret — between duties, beyond the walls of the tower and the fortress alike. Then the order came, and Thomas had to return to Stonegard Castle. Before they parted, they promised each other they would write. And they kept that promise. The letters truly came. At first glance, they appeared innocent — mere mentions of the weather, of duty, of ordinary days. The words were chosen carefully, crafted not to reveal more than they were allowed to. But between the lines — hidden among remarks about weather and service — there was something else. Tenderness. Memories. And a bond that not even distance could sever. Morning arrived cold and pale. The desert wind had weakened, yet in its wake remained the raw silence that comes just before dawn. The campfire had long since burned out. The embers were covered in a layer of gray ash, and the air smelled of dust, sweat, and something ungraspable. Rowena still sat in the same place, her knees drawn to her chest, her head resting against her arm. She had not slept. Her eyes were closed, but her mind remained awake — half lost in the distance, half in memories. When she finally opened her eyes, a pale pink glow was already smoldering along the horizon of the new day. The walls of Stonegard Castle, which during the night had seemed like part of a dream, were real now. Stone. Impenetrable. Footsteps crunching against gravel pulled her from her thoughts. “Did you sleep at all?” a quiet voice asked. James. She turned toward him and gave a faint smile, tired but grateful. “There was no need. Sometimes it’s enough just to sit.” He sat down beside her. Without a word, he handed her a cup of something warm. It was a simple blend of herbs — bitter, but comforting. Rowena took a sip and let the heat spread through her stomach. “You look pale,” he remarked quietly. “It’s nothing.” For a moment, neither of them spoke. James remained silent. He was not the kind of person who forced words out of others, and she appreciated that. By the time Aaliyah rose, the camp had almost been dismantled. Knights gathered weapons, saddled horses, and extinguished the last traces of dying heat. DaVinci stood at the edge of the camp, thoughtful, his eyes fixed on the horizon. “We’ll reach Stonegard Castle today,” he said as the group gathered around him. “If nothing stops us along the way, we should arrive shortly after noon. Prepare yourselves.” The sun slowly climbed above the horizon. Golden light flowed across shields, helmets, and the edges of cloaks. The sand shimmered, and in the distance the stone walls of the fortress wavered like a mirage. And yet… they were real. Every step the horses took toward them felt like the beating of a heart. Stronger. Faster. He was there. Close. Stonegard castle was drawing near.
