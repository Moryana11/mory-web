Chapter IX. – Days from ashes

Searching for the Key — James’s Point of View A small boy no older than ten pressed himself against the cold stone wall of one of the forgotten alleys of Herby City. Rain dripped through his hair and soaked the oversized coat hanging loosely from his thin frame. Even in darkness, the city never fell silent. The cries of street vendors mixed with the clatter of horseshoes and the occasional shout of someone who had lost money, food— or simply their illusions. James held a piece of soaked bread tightly in his hands, something he had found behind one of the market stalls. He sat down in the shadows where nobody would notice him and quickly devoured his first meal in two days. The cold stung his ears, but he tried not to make a sound. Because he already understood one thing: the weak were swallowed mercilessly by Herby. He watched the people passing by, studying every movement and every glance. He learned quickly — who seemed kind, who was dangerous, who might offer a starving child a scrap of food. Even then, vigilance and the determination to survive had already begun taking root inside him, guiding him toward a future he could never have imagined. Suddenly, a rustling sound came from nearby. James froze. A few steps away crouched a black-haired boy perhaps two years younger than him. Half-hidden behind discarded crates, the child stared at him with black eyes filled with hunger and fear. Without a word, James extended his hand and offered him half of the bread. The younger boy hesitated. Then nodded. And sat beside him in the shadows. That day, they became brothers. James watched over him, taught him how to survive, where to hide, how to find food quickly. The smaller boy was slower, clumsier, easily distracted— yet James never became angry with him. One evening, after rain had left the streets slick and nearly empty, they spotted a drunken man collapsed in one of the narrow alleys. The younger boy stopped immediately, eyes lighting up. He saw opportunity. Something valuable enough to trade for real food. He crept toward the man, reaching for the coin pouch hidden inside his coat. “Wait,” James whispered urgently, his voice low but sharp. “It’s not safe. Stay behind me. I’ll go first.” The younger boy frowned at him for a moment, but the determination in his eyes remained unchanged. He crouched beside the drunk man and quickly reached into the coat. Then, just as he tried to run back toward James proudly holding his prize— he tripped. And fell hard against the stones. The sound echoed through the silent alley. The man stirred and opened his eyes. His furious gaze locked instantly onto the younger boy, and his hand shot out, grabbing him hard. The child struggled desperately to break free, but the drunk man was far stronger. James froze. And in that moment, he knew he had to act. There was no time to hesitate. “No!” the older boy shouted as he threw himself at the man. The drunk released the younger child and lunged at James instead. The blows came hard and painful, but James refused to back down. There was more than fear in his eyes now — there was defiance. And that defiance caught the man’s attention. Laughing, the drunk seized James by the throat and lifted him into the air. James still tried to kick him despite the pain tearing through his body. Eventually, the man made a decision. He grabbed both boys tightly and dragged them through the streets into a filthy tavern where the air reeked of dampness, sweat, and cheap alcohol. Still clutching the children, the drunk staggered toward a table hidden in the shadows. A hooded man sat there. Most of his face remained concealed, only his eyes briefly glinting beneath the weak light of a nearby lantern. The drunk leaned heavily against the table and jerked his head toward the boys. “I heard,” he slurred through alcohol-thickened speech, “you’re looking for a pair of boys.” The hooded man slowly raised his eyes and gave a short nod. The drunk leaned closer with a grin. “I’ve got two boys for sale. Young, but capable. Feed them a little and you can do whatever you want with them.” The hooded man tilted his head slightly. “How much?” he asked calmly. The drunk thought for a moment before smirking. “Five gold coins.” The hooded man slowly shook his head. “They aren’t worth that much,” he said coldly, emotionless. “I’ll offer eight copper coins. Five for the older one. Three for the younger.” The drunk’s expression twisted immediately with anger. “What? The older brat fought me in the street!” The hooded man merely gave the faintest smirk, his gaze unmoving as it studied both boys. Then, after a moment, he tossed eight copper coins onto the table. “Take the money,” he said calmly, his voice utterly without mercy. “Or keep the boys.” “Fine. The little bastards are yours now.” The drunk snatched the coins greedily and headed toward the bar for another bottle without looking back even once. James and the younger boy were helpless. But James remembered everything. Every strike. Every glance. Every sound. The hooded man leaned slightly closer, his eyes still hidden beneath the darkness of his hood. “Listen carefully,” he began calmly, his deep voice firm and uncompromising. “The world you’re entering is nothing like the streets you’ve wandered until now. Here, only those who learn to fight survive. Only those who do not fear pain. Only those who understand their own worth.” James felt his heart pounding rapidly, but the man continued in the same cold tone. “Do not expect mercy. Do not expect anyone to offer you a helping hand. You will be trained. Tested. The strong survive, and the weak die.” The boys stood beside the table, tense and silent. The younger child pressed himself tightly against James while fear mixed with growing dread inside them both. The hooded man smiled only briefly. Coldly. “But don’t worry,” he continued. “If you endure and learn what is necessary, you will become something worthy of attention. Just remember this: survival is a game where pain is the rule and weakness is a luxury you cannot afford.” Then he fell silent, gave a short nod, and stepped back into the shadows, his presence still hanging heavily over the boys. James understood then that their lives had changed completely— and that the path ahead would be cruel, merciless, and filled with darkness. The hooded man rose from his seat, took hold of both boys, and led them through the streets of Herby, which seemed even colder and more hostile beneath the night sky. James held the younger boy’s hand tightly. He could feel him trembling and knew he had to stay strong now— not only for himself, but for him as well. The journey was long and dark. Narrow alleys gave way to abandoned courtyards and ruined gates until finally they arrived before a towering stone gate reinforced with heavy iron bars. The man guided them through the main gate and out of the city, along rocky roads winding between trees and cliffs toward a distant estate. When they arrived, they were not brought through the main entrance. Instead, they entered through a small hidden back door concealed among the trees. They did not go upward into the mansion. They descended. Down a narrow stone staircase leading beneath the estate itself. Someone was already waiting for them there. A mage. And his assistant. The mage was short, overweight, and balding. His face appeared friendly enough, yet his eyes were sharp and cold — the eyes of a viper incapable of mercy. His assistant, silent and vigilant, observed the boys sternly through round glasses. “Welcome,” the mage said cheerfully, though unspoken threats lingered beneath his voice. “I hope you’re prepared.” Slowly, he approached them and began inspecting them as though they were not children, but objects whose value and potential needed careful examination. His hands tested muscle firmness, the flexibility of joints, the tension within their bodies. “Hm… they’re thin,” he muttered while dragging his fingers along the older boy’s arm. “They don’t look like they’ll survive what awaits them for very long. We may need to purchase replacements if this fails.” The hooded man remained standing nearby, silent and motionless, observing every movement the mage made. “The older one,” the hooded man finally said calmly, “fought a drunken man in the street. We’ll see how much he can endure.” The mage narrowed his eyes and slowly studied James, then the younger boy beside him. His expression remained cold and analytical, completely devoid of sympathy. “Good,” he muttered quietly. “We’ll see.” That very night, the boys were taken into the laboratory beneath the estate. It was a cold chamber filled with metal tables, cabinets of strange instruments, and dim greenish lamps casting weak light across the walls. The air smelled of chemicals and damp stone. The first months of experimentation were brutal. Every day began before sunrise and ended long after nightfall. The mage tested them constantly — sometimes physically, sometimes psychologically. Every failure, every weakness, every trembling movement was carefully recorded onto parchment scrolls. Dark rituals were performed on them and the other children trapped there every single day. Before and after the rituals, the boys were forced to drink various potions. Some clouded their minds. Others burned through their veins like fire. Some accelerated their hearts so violently they could hear each heartbeat pounding like drums inside their skulls. James was often chained in cold iron restraints, though he barely felt the chill anymore. The only thing he truly felt was pain. Endless pain. The younger boy suffered the same torment, but his body was smaller, more fragile, weaker. Everything hurt him more. As the days passed, the boys became increasingly exhausted. Their bodies grew covered with bruises, scars, and trembling wounds. And then came the day the mage’s rituals became even darker. The boys watched as more and more children failed the experiments. Small lifeless bodies collapsed onto the stone floor, their faces frozen in the agony they had felt before death. Strange mutations spread across their skin — claws replacing fingernails, bark-like growths covering flesh as though the rituals had tried to transform them into something else……and failed. James learned to focus on only one thing: breathing. When the others descended into panic, he repeated silently to himself: Inhale. Exhale. Inhale. The rhythm of his heartbeat became his refuge. As long as he breathed, he was alive. And as long as he lived, there was still a chance. “You see,” the mage muttered one day while observing the younger boy collapse beneath the strain of another trial, “weakness is not forgiven here.” There was cold fascination in his eyes— the expression of a scientist studying insects trapped inside a jar. Months passed. James could feel the younger boy changing as well. The helplessness of childhood slowly disappeared from him. Only stubborn endurance remained in his eyes. Pain became a teacher, and fear became a tool for survival. The tests eventually ended— or at least the worst of them did. But peace never came. Only change. Instead of shackles, they were given weapons. The daggers were old and dull, their chipped edges tearing flesh more often than cutting cleanly. Every strike hurt their hands. Every fall left another scar behind. From a distance, the mage observed their training and recorded everything onto parchment scrolls — who attacked without hesitation, who defended themselves, who hesitated. “A hired killer is not a knight,” the hooded man often said while the boys struggled for breath. “Honor means nothing to him. He doesn’t seek glory or recognition. The only thing that matters is the result — a dead target and your own survival.” James quickly learned to recognize the rhythm of combat the same way he once learned the rhythm of his breathing. He observed. Studied. He paid attention to footwork, striking angles, weaknesses. At first, the older boys beat him relentlessly. But over time, he became the one others avoided. He was not the largest. Nor the strongest. But he was fast. And above all— calm. The younger boy no longer trembled when holding a dagger. On the contrary, his fingers gripped it with certainty, as though the weapon had become part of his own body. Only raw, untamed fury remained in his eyes, igniting every time he smelled blood. He was James’s opposite in every way. Where James chose patience, the boy attacked. Where James observed, the boy acted. There was something feral inside him, something unwilling to submit to discipline. While James learned to kill out of necessity, the younger boy had already begun finding twisted satisfaction in it. And the hooded man noticed. Instead of suppressing it— he smiled. Because this was exactly what he wanted them to become: two daggers. The training was merciless. At night, they were awakened by commands to kill the shadows the mage summoned — illusions of humans, beasts, demons. Every movement had to be lethal. Every strike precise. Those who hesitated were whipped or denied food. Those who rebelled disappeared. No one ever asked where they went. In time, the boys themselves became figures dressed in black cloaks and black masks, moving silently like specters. The hooded man taught them that killing alone was not enough. They had to remain unseen. Leave no trace. Wait patiently. “Killing,” he would say while pacing around them with his hands clasped behind his back, “is not an art. The art is making sure nobody ever realizes it happened.” And within the silent underground halls where the screams of dying children once echoed, only the sound of weapons striking wood remained now— and the quiet breathing of those who had learned how to survive. James was becoming one of the best. Not because he wanted to. But because he no longer knew how to be anything else. Years passed. The boys became young men. They moved with a grace even seasoned swordsmen would envy — silent, precise, cold. One day, the hooded man summoned them to him. He stood beside a stone table in a chamber so quiet that the dripping of water from the ceiling could be heard echoing through the darkness. Resting upon the stone lay four black daggers. Their blades gleamed strangely, as though they consumed light rather than reflected it. James and his brother stood opposite him. Neither asked questions. Both already knew the time had come. The hooded man slowly ran his fingers along one of the blades, almost like a caress. “You have trained for a long time,” he said, his voice muted behind the mask. “You learned how to survive. Now we’ll see whether you’re capable of more than simply living.” He extended his hand. Two daggers for each of them. The cold metal felt heavier in their palms than expected. “Tonight, you will kill for the first time. Not for me,” he continued quietly, “but for yourselves. For the past that created you.” James did not move. He only tightened his grip around the hilts. His brother, however, smirked. That familiar predatory smile appeared at the corner of his mouth. “Who?” he asked. The hooded man paused briefly. “The man who sold you,” he answered simply. The air itself grew heavier. Even without words, they understood. That night, they would not kill because of an order. They would kill because of what had turned them into monsters. The night they departed was cold. Rain hammered against rooftops while the streets remained empty. Their target lived in a small village beyond the city walls, inside a crumbling shack surrounded by the smell of cheap liquor. He was a broken man now. Once brutal. Now only a shadow of what he used to be. James’s brother smirked as he spotted him through the cracked window. “So this is what we survived for?” he whispered mockingly. “Pathetic.” James said nothing. His hand did not even twitch. He simply watched as his brother crouched low and slipped silently inside. A footstep. A muffled cough. Then— silence. James entered only moments later. Inside, the room smelled of blood and alcohol. The man lay sprawled across the floor while his brother stood above him, raising a dagger for another strike. There was more than hatred in his eyes now. There was fury. The kind that had slept for years and finally awakened — raw, uncontrolled, consuming. It mixed with disgust and deep contempt. His handsome face twisted with rage, teeth clenched tight, breathing ragged. Every breath sounded like a scream of fury forced back down into his chest. And James… simply stood there. Watching. The hatred burning inside his brother’s eyes reflected faintly within his own — not with the same intensity, but like a shadow. Cold. Silent. Without passion. “That’s enough,” James said quietly. His brother turned toward him. “Enough?” he repeated. “I don’t know that word.” The dagger came down again. And again. And again. Of the man who had once sold them, nothing remained except torn flesh and shattered bones. There was no battle in it. No redemption. Only violence returning to the place where it had been born. James watched. He did not try to stop him. He only stood there, breathing— inhale, exhale— telling himself that perhaps this moment was not the end. But the beginning. Outside, the rain continued falling. And when they stepped back into the darkness, the masks covering their faces glistened with water and blood alike. His brother, still breathing heavily, turned toward him. James merely nodded. Hidden beneath the mask, his gaze remained cold and distant, as though everything that had just happened had been nothing more than another stretch of road— not the destination. They decided to return to the Crimson Estate through the rear entrance. Old dust-covered doors hidden within the back gardens offered a discreet path unseen by servants or guards. Rain drummed softly against rooftops, leaves, and brick while their footsteps remained nearly silent— like shadows returning home. The familiar underground chamber awaited them beneath the estate. The door creaked as they entered, their arrival announced by nothing except the sound of their own breathing. The hooded man sat at the table. The mask concealed his expression, but his presence remained just as piercing as the first night they had met him. “The mission is complete,” James said quietly, though firmly. His brother stood beside him, breathing still uneven, eyes still burning with the remains of fury. The masked man leaned forward slightly, resting his hands upon the table while silently observing them for a long moment. “Good,” he finally said, his voice calm yet filled with authority. “This was only the beginning. A second task awaits you.” James’s brother straightened immediately, one hand instinctively falling toward his dagger. “A second task?” he asked, rough tension creeping into his voice. His eyes met James’s. And within the silence that followed, both of them understood one thing: the night was far from over. The masked man tilted his head slightly, his tone remaining cold and uncompromising. “Yes. The first task was merely a test.” “This…” He paused. “…is the real mission.” James nodded, his expression calm and unreadable, betraying no emotion. His brother, however, tightened his grip around the dagger’s hilt, already prepared to face whatever came next. “Everything you’ve done so far was only preparation,” the masked man continued. “Now you must prove something more. This is… a test of character.” Silence filled the chamber beneath the estate. The atmosphere felt tense, heavy— like the air before a storm. Both brothers understood that returning to any kind of normal life was no longer possible. And that the real game was only beginning now. His brother scoffed softly, voice edged with irony. “We’re ready,” he muttered. “Though I hope this time we won’t have to drown ourselves in the blood of strangers.” The masked man chuckled quietly. The sound was deep, cold, echoing against the stone walls of the underground chamber. “No,” he finally answered, his voice calm yet piercing. “This time… it won’t be a stranger’s blood.” The masked man straightened and gave a slight nod. “Follow me.” There was no room for refusal in his tone. The brothers followed him down a narrow staircase descending deeper beneath the estate. The stairs ended before a long damp corridor lined with dark stone walls covered in dust and moss. Smaller side passages branched away from it — narrow, twisting tunnels that seemed to lead nowhere… or perhaps everywhere. The masked man moved with certainty. He knew every turn, every hidden corner of the underground labyrinth as though the place itself belonged to him. The brothers remained close behind him, though their eyes wandered toward the shadows lurking inside the side corridors more often than toward his back. Each narrow passage felt like a trap. Every corner carried the promise of danger waiting to emerge from darkness. After several long minutes, when the tunnels seemed endless, they finally reached a larger chamber. Wide doors stood ahead. Beyond them opened a training hall. Torches illuminated the stone floor and wooden practice dummies prepared for combat exercises. High above them stretched a balcony where several masked figures stood motionless, silently observing them with interest. The masked man stopped walking. His deep uncompromising voice shattered the silence. “This is your true trial. You must face one another. The one who survives will become part of the Crimson Order.” He paused briefly. “The one who falls… will disappear forever.” The brothers remained frozen in place. Their breathing quickened. Their eyes met— and within those looks lingered shock, disbelief, disgust… and hidden terror. “What…” his brother whispered hoarsely. “What do you mean?” His voice trembled. “Us… against each other?” The masked man merely nodded without moving. “Yes. Your skills, your strength, your determination — all of it must be proven now. The one who survives will become one of us. The other… will not.” James stood perfectly still, as though the masked man’s words were simply another stage of a path he had always known he would walk. His younger brother, however, tightened his grip around the dagger. His heart pounded violently in his chest. “That… that’s impossible,” he breathed out. “We can’t… he’s my brother…” “That is irrelevant here,” the masked man answered coldly. “Once you step onto the training ground, each of you stands alone. The rules are simple: survival means becoming part of the Crimson Order. Decide accordingly.” Above them, the masked figures on the balcony watched in silence. Their presence felt like silent witnesses to the merciless fate now hanging over the brothers. James slowly turned toward his younger brother. His face remained calm— unnaturally calm— as though every storm inside him had finally gone silent. And in his eyes was something the younger boy never expected to see: determination, coldness, and quiet compassion all at once. “Kill me,” James said softly. “And live.” His brother stared at him in disbelief. “What?” he blurted out. His trembling hand loosened, and the dagger slipped from his fingers onto the stone floor. “Listen to me,” James continued, even calmer now. “If you want to live — if you want any chance at all — you have to do it. This is the price.” His voice carried no desperation. No pleading. It sounded final. As though he had already calculated long ago exactly what needed to be sacrificed. Something twisted painfully inside the younger brother’s chest — rage toward the masked man, betrayal, and beneath it all, gratitude for everything James had done for him. Above them, the masked man leaned slightly forward, as though savoring the scene unfolding before him. “Decide,” he called down coldly. “Time waits for no one.” The younger brother looked back at James— at his calmness, at the silent command hidden within his eyes. There was more than a challenge in that gaze. There was reassurance. A promise that somehow everything would be alright. “Why…” the younger boy whispered at last. “Why are you doing this?” James never looked away. “To protect you,” he answered simply. “So you can become something more than a victim in their games.” He paused. “Do it, and live. If you don’t… we both die for nothing.” Someone on the balcony laughed softly— brief, almost inaudible. And time itself seemed to narrow into a single moment. The younger brother took a step forward. Slowly. Hesitantly— as though every movement caused him pain. One step. Then another. Then a third. And between them stretched silence so complete that only dripping water and the beating of two hearts could be heard. James did not move. He stood perfectly still, his daggers still resting at his sides, making no attempt to defend himself. His gaze remained calm. Accepting. Ready to die, if it meant his brother would survive. The younger brother raised the dagger— his hand trembling violently. He took one final step… and then suddenly sprinted forward. The scream that escaped his lips was a mixture of despair and rage directed at the entire world. He charged straight toward James— but at the very last instant, he changed direction. Instead of his brother, he threw himself at the masked man. The dagger flashed in the torchlight. Then struck. Once. Twice. Again. And again. The masked man never even had time to scream. Caught completely off guard, unable to comprehend that the rules of his own game had just collapsed before him. Blood splattered across the stone floor, droplets striking the ground like echoes of a decision that could never be undone. The younger brother slowly straightened, dagger still clenched in his hand while his breathing gradually steadied. Then he laughed. A deep, raw laugh filled with fury and release. At first short and mocking— then louder, until the entire underground training hall echoed with his defiance. “You think I’ll play your game?” he shouted toward the masked figures watching from the balcony, his voice burning with anger and bitter amusement. “You want me to murder my own brother just to survive?” He raised the bloodied dagger. “To hell with you all!” His voice cracked with emotion. “I’d rather die too!” The masked figures above froze. Some gripped their weapons tightly. Others simply stood motionless, shocked and stunned. Silence fell over the chamber so heavily it felt tangible. James remained frozen in place, eyes widened in disbelief, breath trapped inside his lungs. And then— laughter echoed from the balcony. Deep. Genuine. Amused. The man who had overseen the entire trial leaned forward over the railing, laughing openly. “I like you boys,” he called down with obvious satisfaction. “You’re like a pair of daggers. One is useful…” He grinned beneath the shadows. “…but two are better.” “You both live.” The atmosphere shifted instantly. The unbearable tension dissolved into something strange— heavy disbelief. The younger brother drew a deep breath. His laughter slowly faded, leaving behind only relief mixed with pride. The air beneath the estate trembled faintly. The masked man’s words still echoed through the chamber while the brothers stood there silently, unable to fully comprehend what they had just heard. “You may go,” the man added calmly, a tone disturbingly unfitting beside the corpse of his dead companion. “Return to your quarters. Rest.” A brief pause followed. “Tomorrow begins a new day.” The brothers bowed and left through the corridor. Their footsteps echoed hollowly through the stone halls while water dripped from the ceiling in rhythm with their breathing. Neither of them spoke. The silence between them was heavy, filled with everything left unsaid— fear, guilt, and an incomprehensible calm after the storm. When they entered the sleeping quarters, the air felt heavier there than anywhere else underground. Other young men sat upon the beds — exhausted, hollow-eyed, all dressed in the same dark clothing. The moment they saw the brothers, they lifted their heads. And suddenly, the room fell silent like a grave. “How…” one of them breathed out shakily. “How is that possible? We… we had to kill our partners.” James looked at him quietly, without pride. “The trial isn’t about blood,” he said slowly. “It’s about choice. About whether you allow yourself to be broken… or whether you keep standing.” The words struck the room like stones falling into still water. Confusion flickered across the others’ faces. Then anger. Then guilt. And then it happened. One of the young men suddenly exploded in rage. His hand trembled violently, his expression breaking apart somewhere between despair and madness. “That’s not fair!” he screamed. He drew a dagger with shaking fingers and charged directly toward James. “You said it’s about choice?” he shouted hysterically. “Then now I’ll make one of my own!” James barely had time to react. He only saw his younger brother move in front of him— a blur of motion, a flash of steel— then impact. The dagger plunged deep. “No!” James shouted, catching him before he collapsed onto the floor. Blood spilled through his fingers— warm, terribly real. “No… no, no, not this—” The younger brother trembled weakly, lifting his eyes toward James. Everyone around them froze. No one moved. Time itself seemed to stop. “You mustn’t… blame yourself,” he whispered weakly, though his gaze remained clear. “You were willing to die to protect me.” A faint breath escaped him. “I’m only… returning the favor.” James felt his heart pounding violently inside his chest. But something changed. The world around him began dissolving. Torchlight fractured. Shadows melted and lost their shape. “What…?” James breathed. But his brother only smiled softly. Then he stood up— even though his body should not have been capable of it— and looked directly into James’s eyes. “You have to go, James,” he said quietly, gesturing toward a door that emerged from the darkness as though it had materialized from nothing itself. “This… wasn’t real,” James whispered. “It was,” his brother answered calmly. “These are your memories.” His smile softened. “But there’s nothing to regret.” A brief silence followed. “Now go,” he whispered. “And live.” James looked toward the door— then back at his brother. The younger man smiled one last time. Peacefully. Almost at peace with everything. James took a step forward and passed through the door. Searching for the Key — Aaliyah’s Point of View Aaliyah lay upon silk sheets, their cool softness contrasting sharply with the burning heat still lingering inside her. Morning was muted. Light barely pushed through the fabric walls of the tent. The air smelled of incense. He was gone. As always. William. Beautiful and dangerous like lightning itself. His deep brown eyes had always been cold as ice. At night, they whispered her name softly and insistently, like a prayer no one else was allowed to hear. By morning, they looked at her as though she were nothing more than an empty vessel existing only so he could pour his desire into her before casting her aside. Memories of their first meeting struck her again. It happened in Vienta during the festival season, when the streets pulsed with color, spice-filled air, and the rhythm of drums. The city lived in joyful chaos, but Aaliyah had felt then that her own life was about to turn upside down. William had arrived under orders from his lord to seek out the city’s fire mages. He needed their power and knowledge for a mission deep within the unforgiving desert. The first time their eyes met felt like the collision of two blazing worlds. William’s controlled charisma and cold seriousness clashed against Aaliyah’s wild energy and the fire that burned around her like an untamed storm. And yet, in that first moment, something deeper than mere cooperation was born between them. “There’s fire burning inside you,” he said in a low voice that allowed no resistance. “The kind of heat that could burn everything around it. I want to control that flame — tame it, bind it, use it however I wish.” His gaze darkened. “This isn’t only about power. It’s about power over you. Over whatever burns inside you. You’re wild… untamed…” A faint smile touched his lips. “And I’ll decide when and how that fire erupts.” His words burned hotter than any flame. Aaliyah flushed crimson, but William only smiled — softly, dangerously, like a hunter who had just claimed his prey. Their nights became like secret rituals that both destroyed her and kept her alive at the same time. William’s body was strong and muscular, every movement precise and uncompromising. He touched her with irresistible certainty — one hand sliding along the pulse in her throat while the other gripped her waist firmly. His lips traced against her skin. His teeth grazed softly, possessively, as though he wanted to burn his mark directly into her flesh. Aaliyah opened herself to him completely — both her wildness and her fragility. Their movements melted together into a fierce, chaotic dance where every breath carried anticipation and fear, every touch pulsed like a storm that could no longer be stopped. His kisses transformed pain into pleasure. But morning always came. And when it did, William was gone. Or worse — he remained beside her like a cold shadow, distant and unreachable. Aaliyah wrote him letters, yet the only answer she ever received was silence, and that silence burned her far more deeply than any cruel words could have. He never kissed her. The one time she dared press her lips against his, he pulled away coldly and whispered: “I don’t like that.” The words struck her like ice, shattering every fragile hope she carried inside herself. And still… somewhere deep within her heart, she kept believing that one day he would change. Beside her on the pillow lay a note. Simple. Cold. Emotionless. “It’s over.” The words felt like a quiet execution. How could she love someone who discarded her so easily? Someone who never truly saw her? Someone who took from her endlessly, yet never gave her anything beyond fleeting passion? And despite all of it, she still clung to the fragile hope that someday she could change him. Loneliness wrapped around her like iron chains. She sat up slowly, breathing heavily, yet no tears came. Only silence. Heavy emptiness surrounding her like cold fog. “I loved him,” she whispered into the emptiness. No one answered. “I wanted him to see me as a person. Not as a toy he reaches for whenever he grows tired of war.” And then— a figure appeared in the corner of the tent. Dark. But not frightening. James. His voice was quiet, steady, filled with gentleness. “Aaliyah… I love you. I expect nothing in return. I would never want to control your fire.” His eyes softened. “I’ll protect you, even if you don’t want me to. Because real love means staying… even when we aren’t certain, doesn’t it?” Aaliyah froze. His words reached deep inside her, shattering the ice surrounding her heart. James leaned closer, his gaze full of sincerity and warmth. Gently, he touched her cheek. Then kissed her softly— lightly, yet with enough tenderness and passion to set her entire heart ablaze. And just as quietly as he had appeared, the shadow of James slowly faded away, dissolving into the morning light and leaving behind only calmness… and the feeling of new possibilities. Aaliyah remained sitting on the bed, her breathing gradually slowing, her heart suddenly feeling lighter. She finally understood how many emotions she had wasted on William. How much of herself she had sacrificed for a love that only broke her apart. And now? Now it was time to leave the past behind— and search for her true happiness. Slowly, she rose from the bed, dressed herself in simple clothes, and stepped out through the entrance of the tent with quiet determination. And suddenly— she stood once more within the ancient ruined temple where time itself had stopped. James and Rowena were already waiting for her. Their gazes were calm and steady, filled with silent understanding and support. Rowena stood with her hands gently clasped together, her expression peaceful yet radiating quiet strength and wisdom. Beside her, James felt like a protective shadow, his dark eyes glowing with unwavering devotion. Aaliyah felt something inside her begin to change. A new strength slowly awakened within her, while their presence wrapped around her like a warm cloak of hope. For the first time in a very long while— she was no longer alone.
