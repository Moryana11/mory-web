Chapter XIV. – Echoes in the water

The tent was spacious and shaded by thick fabric that softened both the light and the heat. Inside, the air carried the scent of cinnamon and incense. The floor was covered with soft carpets, and blankets and cushions had been prepared for them. With a relieved sigh, Aaliyah removed her cloak and sat down. Rowena stretched before collapsing onto the nearest cushion. “Finally… peace,” she murmured, closing her eyes. Her voice sounded tired, yet content. After the heat and sand of the journey, even such a simple luxury felt precious. James remained standing near the entrance, his gaze sweeping across the surroundings. “Get some rest for now. I’m going to see what’s happening around here — and whether anyone has heard anything unusual.” Aaliyah looked at him with a trace of concern but said nothing. She knew nothing would stop him. And just like him, she could feel that something was smoldering beneath the surface of the oasis. After he left, silence settled over the tent for a while, broken only by the muffled sounds of the marketplace outside. Rowena opened one eye and spoke. “You said you like coming back here. Does that mean you grew up around this place?” Aaliyah smiled faintly, brushing her fingertips along the edge of the carpet. “Not exactly here. I lived in a nearby city called Vienta. A beautiful, exotic place — azure sea on one side and a golden desert on the other.” She paused for a moment, as though hearing the echo of ship bells and curtains rustling in the wind somewhere deep in her memory. “The oasis was where we came to trade, but also where we came to find peace.” Rowena laughed quietly. “So you kept returning here for the silence? I don’t believe that for a second. You usually go looking for adventure, not peace. Unless there’s some handsome man here named Silence.” Aaliyah frowned dramatically before bursting into laughter. She tossed a cushion at Rowena. “Oh, I’ll show you a handsome man!” Rowena merely pushed the pillow aside lightly and shook her head with a grin. “Am I wrong? Every time we went somewhere together, men practically glued themselves to you.” Aaliyah chuckled and covered her face with one hand. “That wasn’t my fault! I just seem to attract them somehow. That’s not exactly a crime.” Rowena leaned closer, her eyes sparkling with amusement. “I never said it was.” Suddenly, a strange feeling washed over Rowena, and her laughter died instantly. Aaliyah noticed the sudden change in her expression and leaned toward her. “Did something happen?” she asked carefully. Rowena shook her head, trying to keep her expression calm. “Nothing… I just get a strange feeling sometimes.” Aaliyah nodded quietly. “Maybe we should lie down and get some rest.” Rowena simply nodded, leaned back against the cushion, and slowly closed her eyes. The orb inside her bag pulsed faintly with dim light. As Aaliyah stared up at the ceiling of the tent, their breathing gradually fell into rhythm with the distant sounds of the marketplace, and before long, both women drifted to sleep. Outside the tent walls, the daylight slowly dissolved into the amber glow of dusk. The sun sank behind the sea of sand, and the oasis gradually grew quieter. Meanwhile, James walked through the marketplace, his eyes searching for signs of unrest. Harim moved a short distance ahead of him, guiding him through the maze of stalls until they reached a small stone temple hidden beneath an overhanging cliff. From the outside, it appeared unremarkable, but once they stepped inside, they found themselves in a chamber bathed in blue-green light. “This is the Spring of Knowledge,” Harim said softly. “Long ago, priests came here seeking guidance. Now only the old — and those who still believe water holds memory — remain.” At the center of the chamber stood a small stone basin. When James leaned over it, he saw his own reflection in the water… and then the image changed. Instead of his eyes, another pair stared back at him — eyes without lenses. James straightened abruptly, breathing hard. Harim caught him as he staggered. “What did you see?” “A warning,” James answered hoarsely. James wandered through the streets of the oasis. Twilight painted the tent walls in shades of gold and crimson, but he barely noticed. His thoughts were elsewhere — the image of those lensless eyes from the temple pool kept returning to him. His chest tightened, and a cold sweat ran down his back. By the time he returned to the tent, night had already fallen. Lanterns hanging from the tent peaks swayed gently in the evening wind. He stepped inside and hesitated — it was silent. Aaliyah was just waking up. Sitting on the edge of her blanket, she looked around as though trying to remember where she was. When she saw him, she straightened immediately. “We need to move on,” he said without preamble. “Tomorrow morning. At first light.” Aaliyah frowned in confusion. “Why so soon? We still haven’t figured out what we’re supposed to do with the orb.” James looked at her seriously. “There’s no time for that right now,” he said curtly. “I’ll explain everything tomorrow, once we leave this place. For now, go back to sleep.” Aaliyah frowned. “Are you sure we shouldn’t talk about it now? Should I wake Rowena?” “No,” James answered immediately. “Let her sleep. We’ll all need our strength for the journey, and we both know Rowena has the least of it.” Both of them looked toward Rowena. She had been asleep for some time now, yet the exhaustion had not left her face. Her skin was paler than usual, her lips cracked, and dark shadows lingered beneath her eyes. Aaliyah took a slow breath, her expression softening for a moment. “You’re right,” she nodded quietly. “She needs the rest.” After a brief pause, she added, “Where are we going, exactly?” “To Stonegard Castle,” he answered without hesitation. “The headquarters of the Stonegard resistance forces is there. We need to warn the command and learn more about the orbs.” Silence followed. James turned away from her and stared into the darkness. Deep down, he hoped Rowena would survive the journey to the castle — though he strongly doubted she would.
