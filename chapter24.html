Chapter XXIV. – Between the walls

The stone gates of Stonegard castle slammed shut behind them with a heavy boom. James, Aaliyah, Rowena, and several knights arrived in the main courtyard. Sunlight broke against the rooftops of the towers, casting sharp shadows across the walls, while the stone paving beneath their feet echoed under the weight of horses, footsteps, and armor. Others were already waiting there. The knights of Stonegard stood in perfectly ordered ranks, composed and disciplined — yet the moment their eyes fell upon the trio, the silence subtly changed. Every gaze turned toward the entrance gate, tension visible in rigid shoulders. One look was enough for everyone to understand that something had happened. Something very bad. DaVinci’s firm voice carried across the courtyard. “The three of them and I will report to the commander of the knights immediately,” he announced, gesturing briefly toward them. “And to the High Mage.” The activity around them dissolved into a muted blur — barking orders, rattling weapons, the strike of boots against stone, fragments of hurried conversation. But Rowena barely noticed any of it. She was not looking at DaVinci. She was searching for the face she had missed so desperately. And finally, she saw it. He stood at the far right edge of the formation among the others, and yet somehow apart from them all. Thomas held his spear firmly in his right hand, its tip pointed toward the sky. A longsword swayed at his waist, half-hidden in shadow. His posture was straight, silent, prepared — as though he himself were part of the stone wall that had suddenly decided to breathe. When he noticed Rowena, surprise flickered across his face before he suppressed it instantly. Rowena moved. She stepped beside James and Aaliyah and followed DaVinci without a single word. Yet she could feel Thomas’s gaze on her back. Evening came, and the air slowly cooled. The shadows of the gate stretched across the sand beyond the walls, and the distant dunes shifted beneath the quiet movement of the wind. Thomas stood watch, motionless, his eyes fixed there — on the desert and the land beyond the gate preparing itself for sleep. Then something foreign appeared within that picture. Movement. Something that did not belong to the stillness of evening — fast, purposeful. And it was coming closer. Thomas instinctively reached for the spyglass and raised it to his eyes. The image sharpened, and the uncertain silhouettes became clear forms: two knights on horseback, riding hard straight toward the gate. Blue cloaks streamed behind them in the wind. He recognized them immediately. Knights serving under Commander DaVinci. Thomas froze. He kept the spyglass to his eyes for another moment, as though hoping he was mistaken. He was not. If they were returning this quickly and without stopping, it could mean only one thing — something had gone wrong. And not just slightly. He lowered the spyglass and wasted no time. Turning sharply, he strode away from the battlements, his pace becoming a run within seconds. Heavy boots thundered across stone as his cloak whipped behind him. He climbed the narrow stairs into the watchtower where his friend was stationed. William sat beside a small table beneath a narrow window, leaning against the wall, his rune-covered shield resting nearby. He lifted his head the moment he heard the approaching footsteps. “What’s happened?” he asked calmly, though alertness had already entered his eyes. Thomas stopped only long enough to catch a breath. “Two knights. Blue cloaks. Riding straight here,” he said shortly. Without another question, William rose to his feet. He grabbed his shield, slung it across his shoulder, and headed for the stairs. Thomas followed immediately behind him. They descended from the walls at speed, taking the steps two at a time. The stone slipped beneath their boots, but neither slowed. Guards turned to watch them pass, instinctively sensing the urgency and clearing the way. They reached the gate moments before the riders. Thomas returned to his position, spear held firmly in hand, while William stood beside him with shield and sword ready. Both men looked toward the dunes. The two riders were close now. And with every strike of hooves against sand, it became clearer that the time of peace was over. When the knights reached the gate, they leapt from their saddles. Dust swirled around them from the dunes. “We are messengers sent by Commander DaVinci,” the first said breathlessly. “His group is riding behind us. More slowly.” William’s expression darkened. “Why more slowly?” The second knight removed his helmet. “They’re escorting civilians. They were attacked… by something evil. Not bandits.” He hesitated briefly. “We also encountered undead and some kind of dark ritual in ruins near the eastern dunes.” The silence at the gate grew heavier. DaVinci was not a man who took unnecessary risks. He was an experienced commander, known for acting with calculation and severity when needed. If he had chosen to take civilians with him and return to the castle, it meant the situation was serious — perhaps worse than they were willing to admit. “The ritual was active,” the first knight added quietly. “Dark markings in the sand. Blood. But… it didn’t look complete.” Thomas and William both frowned. William immediately took command. “Take the horses to the stables. Then both of you head to the castle kitchens — food, water, get yourselves together. After that, you’ll give your full report.” The knights nodded and disappeared deeper into the fortress. William turned toward Thomas. “Back to your post. The moment Commander DaVinci arrives, I want to know immediately. I’m going to Commander Hander and High Mage Parsons.” Thomas nodded and headed back toward the battlements without another word, his gaze already fixed once more upon the dunes. Meanwhile, William moved swiftly through the corridors toward the commander’s hall. If Commander DaVinci had interfered with a dark ritual, it could mean only one thing. This was no longer just about rescuing civilians. Something far greater had awakened. Within minutes, orders echoed through the castle halls. Guards moved into position, the gates were reinforced, reinforcements called in from the inner courtyards. The kitchens began preparing food and hot water, the infirmaries were opened. Stonegard Castle awakened into a state it knew far too well. Thomas had returned to his watch. He stood by the gate with his spear resting against his shoulder, eyes fixed on the dunes as he waited. Footsteps behind him warned him before anyone spoke. William returned and took his place beside him. “Commander Hander and High Mage Parsons already know everything,” he said quietly, without preamble. “The castle is on alert.” Thomas gave a slight nod without taking his eyes off the horizon. “Now we wait,” William continued. “Until Commander DaVinci arrives with his group. And those civilians.” There was no nervousness in his voice. Only a heavy certainty. Thomas tightened his grip on the spear. The waiting stretched into the next day. The sun climbed high above the walls, and the coolness of morning gave way to harsh, relentless heat. The sand beyond the gates blazed almost white beneath the light, and the dunes trembled beneath shimmering waves of haze. Thomas remained at his post. The guards changed shifts, orders were quietly exchanged, but he stayed where he was. His gaze fixed on the horizon as though sheer will alone could force the silhouettes to appear sooner. Meanwhile, William moved several times between the battlements and the courtyard. He checked the guard positions, spoke briefly with the mages, made certain the infirmary and healers were prepared. Everything functioned exactly as it should. And still, time dragged on. Every movement among the dunes raised hope — only for the wind to scatter it moments later. Every distant shadow became a rider for an instant… and then nothing more than a trick of the heated air. The tension slowly thickened. It was no longer a sharp grip, but a constant pressure that refused to fade. Then, shortly after noon, Thomas narrowed his eyes. At last, a real silhouette appeared on the horizon. And then another. The shadows multiplied, separating from one another, taking shape. No longer a mirage of heat or light. They were real. Riders. People. Thomas straightened and raised his hand in the prearranged signal. The guards along the battlements immediately sharpened their focus, voices carrying quietly across the stone as orders passed without shouting. The figures approached slowly. It was them. DaVinci and his group. The gates of Stonegard Castle began preparing to open. Thomas and William moved immediately. Without wasting words, they descended from the battlements toward the gate below, their steps firm and certain, weapons ready. Spears and swords stood prepared, shields within reach. Not because they expected an attack — but because experience had taught them to always be ready. At the gate, they positioned themselves along either side in stances that clearly declared one thing: If anything else emerged from the sands, the castle would answer immediately. Thomas stood facing the entrance, his gaze sharp, never ceasing to scan the surroundings. William briefly surveyed the area, checked the guards’ positions, and nodded. The gate opened, and the group rode into the main courtyard. The moment they were safely inside the walls, the guards shut the gates heavily behind them once more. There were clearly three civilians. Most likely two women and one man. The riders dismounted. The horses were drenched in sweat, and the civilians could barely remain standing. Thomas stepped forward slightly and studied the man first. His posture was steady, his movements controlled despite exhaustion. A fighter, clearly — but not a knight. The armor was light and bore no crest. Thomas’s gaze dropped to his weapons. A spear. And a pair of daggers at his waist. His jaw tightened. Not a knight. Not a sworn soldier. More like a hired mercenary. Or worse — a hired killer. A man without a banner and without honor, one who fought and killed for coin. Then his attention shifted to the woman dressed in red. A single glance at her appearance, the way she carried herself, the cut of her robes, and the faint pulse of magic almost invisibly surrounding her was enough. A fire mage from Vienta. That much was obvious. And finally— the second woman. Thomas inhaled sharply. For one brief moment, his heart forgot to beat. He recognized her. Rowena. Her face was pale, dark circles shadowing her eyes. She could barely stay on her feet. Every breath looked as though it cost her more strength than she still possessed. The sight terrified Thomas. He wanted to move. To cross the space between them, pull her into his arms, shield her from the watching eyes, from the weight of the day, from everything. Then he noticed the mercenary lightly supporting her so she would not collapse. A firm hand resting against her elbow. Red flashed across Thomas’s vision. The entire world narrowed to that single hand on her body. That foreign touch that had no right to be there. The fact that she needed to lean on him at all. Anger surged through him. Heavy. Burning. The kind that demanded action. The impulse was sharp. Nearly impossible to restrain. But the discipline of a knight was stronger. He remained exactly where he stood. Straight. Motionless. His hands locked tightly around the shaft of his spear. Duty came before emotion — it always had. Yet his gaze hardened. There was no longer only fear within it. There was resolve. He would learn what had happened, down to the final detail. What had driven her here in such a state. And who this man was — the one touching her. And when the time came — because it would come — he would deal with him as a knight of Stonegard Castle. A man bound by honor and oath. Not as a jealous man ruled by anger. Rowena lifted her head and slowly looked around, as though searching for someone. Then their eyes met. There was tenderness in her gaze, and it calmed him, if only slightly. Then she drew a quiet breath, looked away, and followed Commander DaVinci without a single word. Thomas watched her until she disappeared from sight. No one around them noticed anything unusual. No one except one man. William. William stood motionless, leaning against a pillar with one hand resting on the hilt of his sword and his shield strapped across his back, as though he simply belonged there. He had only been in Stonegard Castle for two years — promoted and assigned there as Thomas’s superior officer. In that time, the two had become best friends. They were bound by far more than service alone. They had fought side by side in battle, trained until exhaustion, laughed in the worst moments, and drunk themselves speechless together in the nearby tavern. William knew Thomas better than anyone. “So… you found her again.” A crooked grin touched his lips. Serious relationships were not his style, but he wished his friend happiness all the same — even if it was not the kind he would ever choose for himself. His gaze drifted back toward Rowena as she slowly disappeared among the ranks of knights, then returned to Thomas. At that moment, he did not need to say anything else. Not today. But later, when they were alone — perhaps in the watchtower during guard duty, or seated at the old table with food and a drink between them — maybe then he would bring it up again. With a faint smile. With the calm possessed only by men who know when to speak and when to remain silent. Then his eyes shifted toward the other visitors. James and Aaliyah. The sight of them together irritated him immediately. To William, Aaliyah had been nothing more than a brief distraction, something he had never taken seriously. And he certainly had no desire for her to make a scene now. As for James — whom he had known for four years — William had never liked him. He considered him a fool hopelessly obsessed with Aaliyah, yet too cowardly to ever tell her so. And now they were traveling together. If there was one thing William truly despised, it was when someone else took his “toys” — even after he himself had discarded them. With a tense expression, he studied the group once more before turning back toward Thomas. Today was Thomas’s day. And because of that, William let it go. DaVinci moved through the castle corridors faster than usual. His footsteps echoed heavily across the stone floor while the trio behind him could barely keep pace. No one spoke. There was no time. Every second felt too long. When the heavy doors of the main hall appeared before them, DaVinci did not slow for even a moment. He shoved them open, and they swung wide with a loud groan. Inside, Commander Hander and High Mage Parsons were already waiting. They sat at a large round table of dark oak, its surface covered with maps, seals, and unfurled documents. Light streaming through the tall castle windows reflected off Hander’s metal armor and cast sharp shadows across his stern face. Both of them were frowning. Hander sat with his elbows resting against the table, his hands clasped before his mouth. When DaVinci entered with the trio, his gaze lifted — heavy, assessing, uncompromising. Parsons sat upright beside him, her fingers resting lightly against the edge of a map. Her expression was calmer, but her eyes betrayed tension. The silence lasted only a few seconds. Then Hander slowly lowered his hands onto the table, and his voice spread through the hall, deep and hard as the castle walls themselves. “So. What happened?” he asked bluntly. “You were supposed to be on your way back to Castleton.” He leaned forward slightly, his gaze locking onto DaVinci. “Not turning around halfway through the journey and racing back here,” he continued coldly, “as though your life depended on it.” For a brief moment, he fell silent. “And who are these three?” he asked sharply. “I see no colors of any order on them… and they certainly don’t look like random travelers.” At last, Parsons lifted her hand from the map. Her gaze was focused, analytical. “You brought them here for a reason,” she said calmly. “So explain it to us.” Every eye in the room returned to DaVinci. He took a breath. For a brief moment he hesitated, but then he lifted his head, his voice calm despite the strain within it. “This morning, a hawk arrived at my camp,” he said. “From Harim. From the Sanctuary Oasis.” At the mention of the name, Parsons straightened slightly, while Hander’s lips tightened into a thin line. “The message spoke of a brutal murder,” DaVinci continued. “Not an attack. A ritual killing.” Only his voice could be heard in the hall. “One of the young guards,” he added more quietly, “changed. Something consumed him and turned him into a dark knight.” Hander’s hands tightened against the edge of the table. The wood creaked softly beneath his grip. “There was more in Harim’s letter,” DaVinci continued. “The dark knight was apparently searching for three travelers, and Harim asked me to help them on the road if I found them.” He glanced briefly back over his shoulder toward the trio standing behind him. “That’s why I left the camp with my company and went looking for them.” He looked back at Hander and Parsons. “We found them before it was too late,” he said. A shadow of anger entered his voice. “That dark knight — Zarveth — caught up to them shortly before we did. He nearly killed them. But they managed to wound him. And while my company distracted him, they were able to drive him back.” He paused for a moment. Then he continued, more slowly this time. “On the way back, we discovered a dark ritual,” he said quietly. “Zarveth was performing it personally. Judging by what the undead were wearing, several knights of Stonegard Castle were sacrificed there.” His gaze hardened. “That is why I returned,” DaVinci finished. “Because this is no longer only the Sanctuary Oasis’s problem. It is a threat to the entire world.” Parsons went pale. Hander lifted his head. Suppressed anger flared within his eyes. For a moment, he said nothing. Then he braced both palms against the table, and when he spoke again, his voice was deeper than before — rough and heavy. “We sent a patrol into the desert,” he said slowly. “To the watchtower near the old trade route.” His jaw tightened. “They never returned. No report. No signal.” He raised his gaze toward DaVinci. “So now we know what happened to them,” he added quietly. “A dark mage killed them.” His hands slowly curled into fists. He did not slam them against the table. He did not shout. The calm radiating from him was far more frightening. But this time, DaVinci did not look away. “No,” he said calmly. That single word struck through the hall harder than a scream. Hander narrowed his eyes. “That was not a dark mage,” DaVinci continued. “Not a man. Not some fanatic playing with rituals.” A brief pause. “It was an ancient Shadow,” he said firmly. “Something born from darkness older than our orders, our vows… even these walls.” Parsons raised an eyebrow, but remained silent. Hander straightened. “I saw what it left behind,” DaVinci continued. “That was not magic a human can wield.” At last, Parsons spoke. “I’ve seen rituals that were supposed to be impossible,” she said calmly. “And there was always someone living behind them.” Hander nodded and slowly exhaled through his nose. Then he gave a brief, dry laugh. “A dark knight. Zarveth,” he repeated. “Interesting name.” His tone dripped with irony. “Dark enough, noble enough… exactly the kind of name people whisper about in taverns.” He straightened again, and his gaze darkened — not with anger, but with hard skepticism. His eyes locked onto DaVinci. “I believe you saw something,” he continued. “I believe our men are dead. But the existence of some ancient Shadow wearing armor?” He shook his head. “Nonsense.” Hander leaned back against his chair. “An enemy made of flesh and blood makes sense,” he said firmly. “A fanatic. A black mage. A fallen knight. All of that is possible. But a Shadow? I don’t believe it.” He tilted his head slightly. “Truthfully, DaVinci,” he added, “did you actually see this creature… or just a man in armor?” DaVinci did not move. When he spoke again, his voice remained calm, without defensiveness. “No,” he admitted. “I never saw him up close.” He lifted his eyes toward Hander. “Before I could reach him, he disappeared. Melted into the shadows as though he had never been there.” A short, dry laugh echoed through the hall. Hander shook his head. “There we have it,” he said with faint mockery. “He disappeared. Melted away.” He settled more comfortably into his chair. “This is no monster,” he continued. “This is a dark mage. Experienced, cautious, perhaps fanatical. Men like that vanish quickly when the situation turns against them.” Parsons slowly lifted her head. She exchanged a brief glance with Hander — a silent agreement without words. “I agree,” she said at last, calmly. “What you’re describing, DaVinci,” she continued, “matches a highly experienced dark mage. Someone like that could work with illusion, shadow, and rituals.” Her voice was steady, rational. “A Shadow would be more convenient,” she added calmly. “But the real enemy is always human.” Hander nodded. “And a human can be found,” he said. “Surrounded. Killed.” Aaliyah suddenly stepped forward. Anger flared in her eyes. “You’re wrong,” she burst out. “That wasn’t—” James was faster. He caught her by the wrist and gave the faintest shake of his head. Aaliyah drew in a sharp breath, teeth clenched, her gaze fixed on Hander. The words she wanted to unleash remained trapped inside her like burning ash. Hander did not miss the movement. His attention shifted from Aaliyah to James. This time, he looked longer. Studying him. Coldly. “Do you know what I see?” he said slowly. He leaned forward onto his elbows. “I see people who are exhausted to the point of collapse,” he continued calmly. His voice remained firm, free of anger. “And a tired mind,” he added, “is easily deceived.” He lifted his chin slightly. “That dark mage frightened you. He played with shadows, rituals, your fear,” he said. A brief pause. “Get some rest,” he finished coldly. “Fear often reveals itself to be nothing more than illusion afterward.” Aaliyah went rigid. Blood rushed into her face until she burned red with fury. Her hands trembled. The fire in her eyes was no longer only anger — it was the sting of deep injustice. James frowned. His jaw was tight, his gaze hard, but he remained silent. He knew any words now would only make things worse. Rowena stood motionless. Shocked. Her eyes widened as she fully realized for the first time that no one here wanted to hear about what they had seen. Parsons gave a calm nod. “I agree with the commander,” she said. “Exhaustion and fear are the worst counselors.” Her voice was even, almost reassuring. DaVinci remained silent. He stood apart, his gaze fixed on the two of them. Not because he did not know what to say — but because he understood that the decision in this room had already been made. And with it, the first mistake. Hander straightened. He placed both palms flat against the table and slowly rose from his chair. His figure cast a long shadow across the maps and the stone floor. “Whoever it is,” he said firmly, “a dark mage, a fanatic, or a fallen knight…” He paused briefly. “…he will pay for the deaths of my knights.” His voice was calm. That only made it more threatening. “I will not allow the blood of a knight of Stonegard Castle to go unanswered,” he continued. “I’ll send trackers. We’ll close the desert roads. Anyone moving through the shadows will be questioned.” His gaze swept across the trio. “We will find him,” he added coldly. “And punish him.” Parsons nodded. “Rituals can be traced,” she said matter-of-factly. “Magic always leaves a mark.” Hander turned toward the doors as though considering the matter settled. “This is our concern,” he concluded. “Stonegard Castle will handle it.” Parsons straightened the parchments before her. Her movements were calm and precise, as though her thoughts were already several steps ahead. “We’ll send a message to Harim,” she said. “We’ll inform him that we’ve investigated the situation.” She lifted a quill. Parsons paused briefly in thought, then nodded to herself. “I’ll send one of my mages,” she continued calmly. “Someone Harim trusts.” She raised her eyes from the parchment. “He’ll explain that there is no reason to panic,” she said evenly. “That the situation is under control and that Stonegard Castle is taking action.” The quill lightly touched the parchment. Hander drew a satisfied breath. “This meeting is over,” Hander declared firmly. “You take care of your people. We’ll handle the rest. Get yourselves back in order. In Stonegard Castle, you are all welcome guests.” His expression hardened slightly. “But let me warn you — I do not want to hear rumors spreading through this castle about Shadows and dark knights. Do we understand each other?” For a moment, silence filled the hall. “Do we understand each other?” Hander repeated, his gaze fixed firmly on the trio. James stepped forward. “Yes,” he answered calmly. “We respect your conditions.” He briefly glanced back at Aaliyah and Rowena. Neither of them spoke. Aaliyah’s lips were pressed into a thin line, anger still burning in her eyes, but she gave a stiff nod. Rowena stood quietly, pale but composed. DaVinci lowered his head almost imperceptibly — a gesture of agreement, not submission. Hander nodded, satisfied. “DaVinci, once your company has rested,” he said calmly, without command in his voice, “you should return to Castleton.” DaVinci gave a formal bow and turned toward the doors. The trio followed him. The heavy doors of the hall slowly closed behind them, muffling the voices inside. The corridor beyond was cold and quiet. “This is madness,” Aaliyah burst out. Her voice trembled with anger. “They don’t see it. They don’t want to see it!” She turned toward James. “Zarveth isn’t just some mage with cheap tricks! We all know that, he—” “Aaliyah.” James’s voice was quiet, but firm. “No!” She pulled away sharply. “Because of their pride, a lot of people could die.” DaVinci stood off to the side, his arms folded across his chest. “You won’t convince them,” he said calmly. “Not today.” Rowena remained silent. Fear lingered in her eyes. Slowly, DaVinci removed one of his gloves and rubbed a hand across his forehead. For a brief moment, he closed his eyes, as though trying to steady his thoughts. When he opened them again, his expression was calm — but harder than before. “Now it’s up to you,” he said quietly. Aaliyah immediately looked toward him. “What do you mean?” “Hander will have me watched,” he answered bluntly. “And once my company has rested, they’ll escort me out of the castle. Politely. With smiles.” James nodded. “So no freedom of movement.” “Exactly,” DaVinci replied. “Which means I can’t help you openly.” He fell silent for a moment before continuing more firmly. “Before I return to Castleton and begin preparing for a confrontation with Zarveth, I’ll stop at the Sanctuary Oasis first,” he said. “I’ll warn Harim personally. Not through polished letters and reassuring words.” Rowena lifted her head. “And after that?” “Then I’ll visit a friend in the Tower of Pure Light,” he answered. “He’ll listen.” Aaliyah drew in a breath. A flicker of hope appeared in her eyes. “So we’re not alone.” “No.” DaVinci shook his head. “But don’t expect Hander, Parsons, or Stonegard Castle to help willingly. We’ll have to be prepared to fight without them.” Silence settled over the corridor. “For now, stay here,” he said quietly. Aaliyah straightened slightly. DaVinci paused for a brief moment before speaking again, his voice more serious now. “Zarveth is wounded,” he said calmly. “And even if he isn’t human, he still needs time to recover.” His gaze moved from one of them to the next. “Rest. Regain your strength,” he continued. “Because afterward, he won’t give us time to rest anymore.” Aaliyah nodded slowly. “And then?” “Then you start searching,” he answered. “Learn everything you can — about the orb… and about how to use it against the Shadows.” For a moment, silence returned. Footsteps echoed through the corridor before anyone could speak again. Calm. Measured. Unhurried. James instinctively turned his head. A servant in dark castle livery stepped around the corner. He gave a slight bow, his hands clasped neatly before him, gaze respectfully lowered. “Commander DaVinci,” he said quietly, “Commander Hander sent me to show you to your rooms.” DaVinci nodded as though he had expected it. The servant then turned toward the trio. “Please, follow me.” He turned and continued down the corridor. Their footsteps echoed once more through the stone halls, muted by the castle’s thick walls. They passed heavy doors, narrow staircases, and tall windows through which the afternoon light streamed inside. Sunbeams broke against the edges of stone and painted pale streaks across the walls where dust drifted lazily through the air. The servant guided them through the corridors until he stopped before a set of massive doors. Turning toward them, he lowered his head slightly. “Before we continue,” he said calmly, “I have been instructed to inform you of Commander Hander’s orders.” DaVinci’s attention sharpened. “You are first to be examined and treated in the infirmary,” the servant continued matter-of-factly. “Afterward, you will proceed to the dining hall. Only then will you be permitted to retire to your rooms.” DaVinci nodded as though he had anticipated this. “That’s acceptable,” he said. “If I’ve been assigned the same room as last time, I can find it myself. I know the way.” The servant bowed lightly once more. “Yes, sir. It is the same room.” DaVinci inclined his head. “Good.” He turned briefly toward the trio. “We’ll speak later,” he added quietly. Then, with only a nod of farewell, he turned and disappeared into one of the side corridors without needing further escort. The servant resumed his duty. “Please,” he said to the trio, “come with me.” He led them to the infirmary. The air there was thick with the scents of herbs, alcohol, and resin. The healers were calm and focused, their movements quick and experienced. They examined and treated every wound carefully — nothing escaped their notice. Once it was finished, the servant led them onward without unnecessary words. The dining hall was bright, flooded with afternoon light. Warm food already waited upon the tables. Simple, hearty meals. Bread, meat, soup, water, and wine. No one disturbed them. Only after they had finished eating did the servant finally lead them to their rooms. “You will be staying here,” he announced calmly. “If you require anything — clean clothes, food, drink — the servants are at your disposal.” He gave a short bow. “You need only ask.” Then he departed, leaving them alone. Silence settled behind the closed doors. Afternoon light streamed through the tall windows, painting warm bands of gold across the floor. The rooms were spacious and airy, furnished with comfortable, heavy wooden furniture. Beneath their feet stretched beautiful handwoven carpets that softened every step. The walls were decorated with rich tapestries depicting battles, ancient castles, and the symbols of Stonegard Castle, their colors still vivid despite their age. A large stone fireplace dominated one wall, currently cold but ready to blaze to life again at any moment and drive away the chill of the castle walls. Vases filled with fresh flowers stood upon tables and windowsills, their delicate fragrance softening the otherwise stern stone atmosphere. Each room also possessed its own private bathing chamber — spacious and built from stone, supplied with heated water and clean linen towels prepared for use. Another quiet luxury ordinary people rarely experienced. At first glance, it was obvious these were not rooms intended for servants. They had been prepared for noble guests. Such comfort was reserved only for those the castle wished not merely to host...but to impress.
