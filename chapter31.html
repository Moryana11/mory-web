Chapter XXXI. – The voice in the stone

One night, after the lights of the castle had dimmed and the bells announced midnight, Brienne slipped into a corridor left unguarded. Her quiet footsteps carried her through dark passageways where the stone walls swallowed sound and the cold bit into her skin like sharpened claws. She stopped before a door at the end of one of the side corridors — old, nearly forgotten, buried beneath cobwebs and grime, as though the castle itself had erased it from memory. Her fingers closed around the rusted iron ring. The door opened with effort. Beyond it, stone steps descended deep beneath the fortress, into forgotten tunnels. She moved downward slowly. The air was heavy. Dead. As though time itself no longer existed there. Only silence. And something breathing within the dark. At one of the arched alcoves, she stopped. The stones there were blackened, covered in soot and ash. Carved into the floor lay the symbol of an eye without a pupil, encircled by runes glowing faintly in the darkness. Brienne fell to her knees. And then— a vast shadow emerged in the corner. So dense, so impossibly black that it could almost be felt pressing against the skin. The darkness folded inward until it formed a single figure. Zarveth stepped from the stone itself. Without a sound. Without breath. Simply there. As though he had always belonged to the darkness beneath the castle. His face was blackened, but his empty, soulless eyes burned like molten gold. In his hands rested a sword forged from black iron, while a dark spear hung across his back. “Well done,” he said softly, though his voice cut like a blade. “They cling to you as though you are their salvation.” A faint smile touched his lips. “And they have no idea you are their ruin.” Brienne lowered her gaze, though a cold gleam flickered briefly within her eyes. Zarveth stepped closer. He reached out and touched her chin. His fingers felt like stone — cold, hard, lifeless. “I must learn more,” he murmured. “The seal is beginning to weaken. Someone no longer holds it firmly enough.” His voice darkened further. “And when it breaks…” The shadows around them seemed to tremble. “When it breaks, our lord Morr'kalil will rise.” His eyes burned brighter. “And the world itself will tear apart.” Brienne nodded slowly. “The castle is open. Parsons and Hander are blinded by their own pride. They believe they control everything. DaVinci can do nothing — they will never allow it.” A shadow of satisfaction crossed Zarveth’s face. “The access to the water chambers is guarded,” Brienne continued quietly, “but the temple itself remains open. The mages carry the keys. Some are exhausted. Some disappointed.” Her lips curved faintly. “Perhaps… ready to listen to another voice.” “Find me someone who can be broken,” Zarveth commanded quietly. “Someone willing to open the way.” His gaze darkened. “And be prepared — soon I will need more from you than whispers.” He turned away, and the shadows behind him folded around his form like veils of living darkness. Then he paused once more. “And Brienne…” The girl lifted her eyes toward him. “When they touch you… when they offer you friendship…” His voice became cold enough to freeze the air itself. “Accept it.” A faint smile twisted across his face. “And then kill them.” And then he was gone. As though the stone itself had swallowed him whole. Brienne remained kneeling there in silence for several moments longer. Then she slowly rose to her feet. The air around her grew colder, but she felt nothing. No guilt. No pride. Only quiet obedience. And patience. There were only Shadows. Their existence belonged to their master alone. And Stonegard Castle had already been infected. No one simply knew it yet.
