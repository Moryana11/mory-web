Chapter XXIX. – Before the gates of light

The gates of Stonegard Castle opened with the heavy groan of iron. The courtyard remained calm, yet watchful — for every newcomer who entered these walls carried a story, and in recent days, stories had become painfully common. Still, the arrival of the girl drew everyone’s attention. She was barefoot, her clothes covered in sand and dust. Her golden hair hung tangled around her face, streaked in places with dried blood. Her skin was pale, nearly colorless, and a thin bruise darkened her temple. But it was her eyes that unsettled them most. Distant. Unfocused. As though she were still staring at the moment the Shadow murdered people she had known, and her mind had never escaped it. The courtyard fell silent. At the center of that silence stood DaVinci, Hander, and Parsons. None of them spoke as the girl was brought before them. “She calls herself Brienne,” one of the knights explained. “We found her in the desert. Her caravan was attacked, and she was the only survivor. She claims they were assaulted by a Shadow.” For a brief moment, DaVinci glanced toward Hander. Hander caught the look — and frowned immediately. His jaw tightened; already he refused to consider where those words might lead. “Brienne,” Parsons addressed her softly, “you are safe here now. Can you tell us what happened?” The girl trembled slightly. Her lips quivered. One of the knights rested a hand gently against her back and motioned for her to step forward. Brienne flinched, but obeyed. At first, she could not answer. She swallowed hard, as though her tongue no longer obeyed her properly. Then she gave a faint nod, and her voice emerged fragile as a spiderweb torn by the wind. “There were seven of us. Healers. My teacher’s name was Evalyne. She raised me… she was like a mother to me.” She paused. Her hands trembled violently. “At night… it came. Like darkness without a face. Like death itself. A creature from the desert, without eyes, without a soul. It burned one of us alive before we could even breathe. The others…” Her voice faltered. “I heard them screaming. Its hands… its voice…” She broke into tears. Not dramatically. Quietly. Painfully. Parsons stepped closer and offered her a handkerchief, which Brienne pressed shakily against her eyes. “I ran,” she whispered. “I ran because I was a coward.” She slowly lifted her tear-filled eyes toward them. “But after I escaped… I prayed for the Light to destroy me.” Her voice trembled painfully. “Because why me? Why was I the one left alive… instead of someone better?” Silence settled over the courtyard. Hander studied her carefully. Fingers that normally remained perfectly still tightened slightly, as though he could feel the girl’s grief deep in his bones. Parsons gave a slow nod. Her voice softened. “Stonegard Castle welcomes you, Brienne. We mourn your loss deeply. And your testimony may save lives.” She paused briefly. “You will rest under our protection. Once the knights sent to verify your story return, we will speak again.” Brienne lowered her gaze and nodded. “Thank you,” she whispered weakly. “Thank you… for believing me.” Parsons watched her for a moment longer before stepping aside and quietly instructing one of the mages nearby: “Take care of her. Make sure she eats and receives clean clothes. No one is to disturb her until she recovers.” As Brienne was led away, the eyes of the courtyard followed her. Guards whispered among themselves about the slaughter she must have witnessed. About how young she was. About the terrible fate that had befallen her. One of the younger squires even wiped a tear from his eye. No one noticed that within Brienne’s lowered gaze, something else flickered for the briefest instant. Something deeper. Something cold. And high above them, hidden upon one of the stone walkways beneath a veil of magic, stood Zarveth. Silent. Unseen. Watching everything. Every gesture. Every glance. A slow smile spread across his face. Perfect, he thought. More perfect than I expected. And the castle had opened its gates not only to a frightened girl. It had opened them to the Shadows.
