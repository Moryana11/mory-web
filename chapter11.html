Chapter I. The Ruins of Turayne

Turayne was once a city that flourished thanks to the Grassroad — an ancient trade route lazily winding between mountains and forests. For centuries, it had been protected by a lineage of powerful mages who devoted their lives to its safety. But on the day of the last High Mage’s funeral, everything changed. That was the day the Shadowmages of the Sylvean Order attacked. War erupted — brutal and short — as the city’s remaining mages fought in vain against the invaders. Turayne fell, and nothing remained of the city but ruins. The stone arches of the old Temple of Tevent reached toward the sky like petrified fingers of gods who had once ruled this land. The entrance hall, once bright and filled with prayers, now stood hollow and consumed by shadow. Time did not flow here — it had sunk its teeth into everything like mold. Dust covered every surface so thickly that each step stirred swirling clouds that seemed to whisper fragments of ancient prayers. The pillars were cracked, some collapsed, others overgrown with black lichens pulsing with their own dark energy. The air carried the stench of rotting flesh and something indescribable that settled on the tongue like ash. From every alcove, statues stared outward — but their eyes had been gouged out, their faces clawed apart. Some were shattered so badly that it was no longer possible to tell whom or what they had once depicted. Among the rubble and broken stone lay bodies — decayed, half-swallowed by the floor itself. Some did not move. Others… moved far too slowly. The undead. Silent and twisted, as though they had long forgotten what their limbs were meant for. They reached out blindly, eyeless, yet still able to sense life. The temple had not spat them out — it held onto them. Everyone remained here. Priests, pilgrims, healers… their souls trapped within the walls, the floor, the shadows. And they waited. The wind crept through the shattered windows, but it carried no fresh air — only cold and the whisper of things long buried that should never have been awakened again. In some parts of the temple, strange echoes lingered, belonging to neither footsteps nor voices. As if something were listening. Something not yet fully asleep. From somewhere in the distance came the crunch of shifting rubble — quiet, yet foreign. A step. Another. Then another. In the dark passage between two collapsed pillars, a silhouette flickered into view for a brief moment. A sliver of light from a broken window fell across the figure as it moved slowly and silently, as though studying every shadow, every crack around it. It looked like someone searching for something — or trying to make certain they were alone. Her long, straight black hair swayed softly with each step, framing her pale skin like a dark waterfall. Her black eyes, deep and calm as the night sky, carried both determination and a quiet sorrow. The blue dress she wore had been marked by travel and dust, yet it still retained traces of elegance. Rowena came from Kastleton, a bustling port city that lived to the pulsing rhythm of ships, markets, and trade. The scent of sea salt mingled with spices, exotic herbs, and fresh fruits brought from distant lands. The streets rang with the voices of merchants offering fabrics, precious jewelry, and mysterious artifacts. Kastleton was a place where cultures, languages, and the fates of people from across the world intertwined. Rowena loved the city with all her heart — it was not merely a place of opportunity, but a home that gave her a sense of safety and freedom. Her family had always been her support. Her parents, experienced merchants, managed the renowned family business of medicinal herbs, many gathered from the surrounding forests. To her, Kastleton was not only the place of her childhood, but the roots that kept her grounded even when her path led far away. When she was eight years old, an old healer visited the city and sensed something extraordinary within her. He realized that Rowena possessed a rare gift for healing magic. After a long discussion, her parents agreed to let their daughter follow her destiny, and the healer took her to the Tower of Pure Light — a prestigious academy where magic was taught in its purest and most powerful forms. Within the Tower of Pure Light, she studied healing rituals, ancient scrolls, and the secrets of the Garden of Peace, all meant to one day make her capable of aiding the suffering. Every day she trained diligently, honing her abilities while carrying both the burden of responsibility and the weight of hope within her. In her hand, she tightly gripped a wand adorned with delicate runes, and at her waist hung a magical book — an ancient tome filled with rituals capable of changing the fates of many. Every step she took through Tevent’s temple felt like sinking into a swamp of memories, a place where past and present merged into one dark current. Every breath she drew echoed like the cries of those who had died here long ago and yet still called for help. And still she continued onward — for the scrolls, for hope. Because even where everything had long since died, there could still lie the key to saving the living. Her mind was sharp, and her heart filled with faith that magic was not merely a weapon, but a light capable of illuminating even the darkest corners of the world. And then she felt it. Not the cold of the wind, but another kind entirely. The kind that crawls down your spine when something without eyes is watching you. Wraiths. Temple priests — shapeless shadows of the past, without faces or voices, yet consumed by an unquenchable hunger to drain the last remnants of light from every living soul. Ten of them. Too close. Too fast. Rowena stepped back, one slow step after another, as the ten wraiths gradually encircled her. Their faceless heads tilted unnaturally, as though studying her every movement, every trace of magic surrounding her. The undead stretched out their hands. Their fingers, long and thin as needles, reached hungrily for her life force. Rowena clenched an amulet with a white crystal in her fingers, cold as ice, meant to grant her strength. But her breath caught in her throat, her heart pounded wildly in her chest, and a desperate thought flashed through her mind. I can’t do this… She knew there were too many of them. Too fast. Too deadly. Suddenly, one of the wraiths lunged forward. Its decayed, translucent body shot through the air with terrifying speed, claws reaching for her throat. And then the world exploded in blinding light. A massive pillar of fire tore through the temple ceiling. A beam of sunlight transformed into a raging inferno that shattered the stone vault like the wrath of an enraged god. Flames instantly consumed the attacking wraith, its ghostly form dissolving within the radiance as though melting into pure energy itself. A scream escaped its mouth, only to vanish beneath the thunderous roar of the fire. The remaining nine wraiths attacked at once, but the flames around Rowena formed an impenetrable circle. Sparks rained across the stone floor, illuminating the dark corners of the temple while the wraiths hissed violently and recoiled from the element that devoured their undead forms. The air filled with the stench of burning. Heat flooded the temple, making the ruin feel strangely alive beneath the blaze. Sparks danced around Rowena, casting trembling light across her tense face. As the smoke slowly began to clear, another figure appeared within the glow of the flames, standing atop a stone pedestal. A woman with shoulder-length red wavy hair. Her eyes were the same deep crimson — piercing and unwavering, as though they carried within them the flames of unquenchable passion and fearless resolve. Her lightly tanned skin only emphasized the expression she wore, one both sensual and dangerous — beauty that was not decoration, but a weapon. She wore red oriental robes, light and flowing, crafted from delicate fabric that moved like hot wind sweeping across desert dunes. Loose where they offered coolness, fitted where they emphasized elegance and beauty, the garments were layered with graceful folds and slits that allowed effortless movement while remaining undeniably alluring. Woven into the fabric was a subtle, almost invisible pattern of waves and wind. At first glance, it was obvious such clothing did not come from the cold northern lands, but from Vienta — the port city at the edge of the Monolith Wastelands. A city where the sun never fully set and scorching winds danced through streets carved from sandstone. Vienta was a city of heat — not only because of its location, but because of what flowed within its people: fire magic. In Vienta, flame was not seen as something dangerous, but as a blessing. Fire was a symbol of life, movement, and transformation. It was mastered from childhood, not as a weapon, but as a living force capable of protecting, empowering, and creating. Their magic was as beautiful as it was destructive, as precise as it was passionate. The city’s greatest temple — the Sanctuary of the Crimson Breath — was devoted to fire as a force of purification. There, students learned not only spells, but discipline and philosophy: that fire, like emotion, must be guided with a steady hand, or it would consume everything. True power did not lie in destruction, but in control, passion, and determination. The garments worn by the mages of Vienta were not merely clothing — they represented balance between strength and beauty, between chaos and calm, between a destructive element and the will to tame it. And she embodied that balance perfectly. Her stance was firm and confident, her gaze sharp and unyielding. Every movement radiated strength and charisma impossible to ignore. She looked like a woman who did not merely welcome trouble, but faced it willingly, with fire in her heart and passion in her soul. “That wasn’t exactly elegant, was it?” a lightly teasing voice said. Rowena stood frozen, her breath still tight in her chest. Her body remembered the threat; her eyes remained fixed on the place where the wraiths had stood only moments ago. And yet… something had changed. That voice, that warmth, that radiant figure shattered the suffocating shadow of fear pressing down upon her. She felt her shoulders loosen slightly. Felt her heartbeat begin to slow. And then she saw it. The remnants of the wraiths — pale, twisted shadows of spirits — were retreating. Not in terror, but cautiously. Respectfully. Some nearly dissolved into the stone walls themselves, while others hissed through soundless mouths, as though uncertain whether they dared reveal themselves again. The hateful energy that had filled the temple moments before suddenly seemed to falter. The presence of the red-haired woman made them hesitate. Perhaps they knew her. Or perhaps… they sensed that she knew them. The woman with crimson hair smiled, gave a casual shrug, then gracefully leapt down from the stone pedestal. She landed with such lightness it seemed less like touching solid ground and more like falling into the rhythm of her own magic. Staff in hand and heat still dancing around her, she began walking toward Rowena step by step, utterly without fear. “Who… who are you?” Rowena breathed, her voice quieter than she intended. Thoughts swirled chaotically through her mind, but one rose above the rest. I’m not alone. “Aaliyah. Lover of trouble. And you are… the one who almost died,” the woman with fiery hair said with a grin, while her staff still smoldered with lingering heat. Rowena swallowed. Her throat was dry, but the corners of her lips lifted slightly. “Rowena,” she replied quietly. Then added, “Thank you… If it weren’t for you…” “You’d be dead, yeah,” Aaliyah agreed with a nod. “But we’re here now. And by the way…” She walked closer and lazily spun her staff in a small circle. “This temple has a lot more of those spirits. Want to look around together? Just… temporarily.” Rowena actually smiled. For the first time in a very long while, the smile was not a mask, but a genuine reflection of something human inside her coming back to life. There was something strangely comforting in the other woman’s gaze. A strength capable of burning — but also protecting. And in that moment, amid the ruins, Rowena realized that this strange woman at her side was not merely a force of fire wrapped in red. She was a friend. “Alright. But I’m warning you — I tend to cause more damage than help.” Aaliyah grinned and winked. “Perfect. Finally I won’t be the only disaster in the room.” Then she leaned closer and added in a whisper, “But if something explodes, I’m blaming you. That’s rule number one.” They wandered through the temple long after the last flames had died away. The stone corridors were cold and silent, broken only now and then by the whispers of wraiths lurking in the darkness. Aaliyah held her staff lightly before her; its reddish-orange glow licked across cracks in the walls, revealing ruined altars buried beneath thick dust and shattered statues. Eventually, they stopped in the main hall. Half the ceiling had collapsed, and pale light filtered through the opening above, mixing with the smoldering glow of the magical staff. The air felt heavy and exhausted — just like them. Without a word, they began searching. Rowena systematically examined alcoves, pushing aside loose stones and testing hidden compartments concealed within the bases of statues. If anything had survived, it would have been hidden away, protected from time and moisture alike. She opened a concealed compartment. Inside was nothing but dust and a few blackened fragments of wood. “Nothing here,” she muttered. Meanwhile, Aaliyah tapped her staff against one of the walls. A hollow thud echoed back. For a brief instant, excitement flashed in her eyes. She quickly shoved aside a stone slab and peered inside. Empty. Aaliyah exhaled through her nose and closed the compartment again. “Magnificent adventure, zero loot. Classic.” For a moment, she stared at the empty hiding place. “If there was ever anything here,” she remarked dryly, “someone faster than us took it. Or smarter.” Rowena looked around the chamber one last time, slowly and carefully, as though checking for a final possibility. Then she shook her head. “There’s nothing left here,” she said quietly. “Only empty hiding places.” Silence settled between them for a moment. Not uncomfortable — shared. Aaliyah sat down on a broken step and pulled a small piece of dried meat from her bag. Without much ceremony, she handed it to Rowena. “So,” she said with a faint smirk, “we searched the temple of a dead god. And the only things we found were dust and a terrible mood.” Rowena accepted the food, the corners of her lips lifting slightly. They sat there together for a while amid the ruins of a temple that no longer had anything left to offer. And yet, it did not feel like wasted time. Eventually, Aaliyah stood up, dusted off her robes, and looked toward the exit where a path stretched between the broken stones into the unknown. “So what now?” she asked, turning her head slightly toward Rowena. “Each on our own… or two disasters together?” Rowena slowly rose to her feet and nodded. “Together,” she answered simply. Aaliyah smiled, this time without teasing. Just content. “Good,” she said. “Then let’s go find trouble somewhere else.” And so their journey began — among the ruins of a shattered temple, between the whispers of the dead and the flames.
