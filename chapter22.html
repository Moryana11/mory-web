Chapter XXII. – Rest before the shadow

The journey continued at a brisk pace. Since the encounter with Zarveth, the land itself seemed to have taken a breath. For a brief while, the group was able to feel a measure of peace. The sun no longer carried the cruelty of midday, and even the wind, though it still brought dust with it, had lost its restless tone. DaVinci, the seasoned knight and commander of the expedition, led them forward in silence. His gaze constantly returned to the horizon, his body carrying the calm of a warrior always prepared for the worst. Behind him rode the other knights, divided into smaller groups. At the center rode Aaliyah, Rowena, and James on borrowed horses. Aaliyah sat upright, but her face betrayed exhaustion. The shadows beneath her eyes had darkened, her gaze had grown unfocused, and her lips were tight with the effort not to drift into sleep. Her magic had completely drained her, and though she never spoke of it, every step of the horse beneath her felt heavier than the last. Beside her, Rowena swayed in the saddle. Her head drooped, and several times she nearly slipped far enough to fall — once caught by James, the second time by Aaliyah. James remained close to them in silence, his body ready to react at any moment. Whether against an unseen threat or to catch one of the women again before they collapsed. The knights maintained their pace. Conversation among them faded away. The silence was broken only by the pounding of hooves and the whisper of wind over the dunes. Meanwhile, the sun passed its zenith, and the sky slowly took on the copper hue of approaching evening. At the front, DaVinci slowed his horse. He turned in the saddle, his gaze settling on the trio. He watched Rowena lean dangerously once again, forcing James to catch her for the third time within a single hour. “Halt!” he called firmly. “We camp here. We go no farther tonight.” One of the knights rode closer to him. “Sir, if we want to reach Stonegard Castle before nightfall, we should keep moving,” he argued quietly. “Every hour works against us.” DaVinci did not look away from him. “And every additional mile could cost them their lives,” he replied calmly, nodding toward the trio. “If they’re going to tell us what’s happening, they need to survive the journey first.” The knight pressed his lips together and nodded. DaVinci looked across his men and pointed at two of them. “You two. Leave immediately. Warn the commander of the knights and the High Mage.” Both knights straightened in their saddles. “Yes, sir.” Without another word, they turned their horses and rode ahead. Their silhouettes quickly shrank until they vanished into the shimmering air above the sand. James helped the women dismount. Aaliyah leaned against her staff and closed her eyes for a moment. The knights began laying out tarps, building fire pits, and securing the horses. They worked quietly and efficiently. DaVinci moved among them with calm, measured steps, every gesture deliberate, every command clear. When he reached the small group, his gaze briefly rested on the women. He saw their exhaustion and asked no questions. He did not need to. It was written across their faces, in every movement they made. “Get some rest. We’re not riding any farther today,” he said simply before turning toward his men. “Split the watches. Silence does not mean safety. No one sleeps too deeply.” “I’ll take the first watch,” James spoke up. DaVinci turned toward him. “No. Rest.” James shook his head. “I’ll be calmer standing guard. When I sleep, I can’t protect anyone.” The knight studied him silently for a moment before nodding. “As you wish. You can take the second watch.” After sunset, Aaliyah sat beside the fire with her staff resting across her lap. The flames cast shadows into her tired eyes, yet that hard glimmer of determination still remained within them. Nearby, Rowena carefully rubbed healing salve over her burned palms while the orb beside her gave off another faint glow. The camp sank into silence. Above them, the stars shone sharply, almost cold. The black sky wrapped around the group like a velvet veil, and the desert around them breathed with a quiet chill. The crackling fire, the soft crunch of footsteps against gravel, and the faint clinking of armor were the only sounds that remained. When the first watch came, James rose and moved to the edge of the camp. His silhouette blurred into the darkness as he stood motionless, eyes fixed on the distance. Before long, DaVinci joined him, wordlessly, as was his habit. For a while, they simply stood there, watching the horizon. The silence between them was not empty — it was the silence of men who had seen too much to speak carelessly. “Do you think he’ll return?” DaVinci asked quietly, without looking away. James remained silent for a moment before answering in a low voice. “Zarveth? He’ll return. But not now. Only when he’s stronger… and when we’re more exhausted.” He tightened his grip around the hilts of his daggers. Silence drifted across the desert once more — but it was not peace. It was the silence before the storm.
